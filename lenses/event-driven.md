# Event-Driven Lens

Reusable knowledge about the **event-driven architectural style** for Java/Spring: communicating between parts of a system by producing and reacting to events rather than calling each other synchronously — what an event should *be*, how producer and consumer are coupled (or decoupled), how an event schema *evolves* without breaking its readers, and, above all, **when communicating by events buys something a plain synchronous call would not**. **The intent of using this lens comes from the consuming skill, not from the lens itself.**

This lens owns the *style* — the topology, the coupling, the event design and its evolution, and the decision to be event-driven at all. It deliberately does **not** own the workflow-consistency machinery a multi-step process needs ([`./saga.md`](./saga.md)), the read/write model split ([`./cqrs.md`](./cqrs.md)), the mechanics of turning a database change into a stream ([`./cdc.md`](./cdc.md)), the aggregate and consistency boundary ([`./ddd.md`](./ddd.md)), or the runtime resilience of a single dual-write/listener/consumer in a diff ([`./spring-production-readiness.md`](./spring-production-readiness.md)). It cross-references those rather than re-deriving them; the "Integration" and "Heuristics" sections below say exactly where each border falls.

## How skills use this lens

This lens has two consumers, used with opposite intents. **`architecture-review`** reads it **evaluatively**, at **system/design altitude** — does the design communicate by events where that genuinely decouples parts that change independently, or has it scattered events across a system whose parts are in fact tightly coupled (a *distributed monolith* wired through a broker)? Is each event designed so its producer can change without silently breaking every consumer? The review-flavored sections below (severity calibration, comment style, integration contract) are written for that design-review intent: *is the event-driven style here decoupling parts that should evolve independently and designing its events to evolve safely — or is it adding asynchrony, eventual consistency, and a broker to a problem a synchronous in-process call already solved?*

That shared use — evaluative for `architecture-review`, generative for `spec-author` (which reads this lens to *decide* whether to communicate by events, what kind of event to publish, and how to version its schema when writing a spec, rather than to judge those after the fact) — is exactly why this knowledge lives in `lenses/` rather than inside one skill: each consumer supplies its own intent, the lens supplies the knowledge. It stays focused on the concrete decisions both intents can act on, not over-generalized. A consuming skill loads it only when the design or change actually involves communicating across a boundary by events as described in "When to Use" below — never just because the words "event," "publish," "broker," or "async" appear in a name, a package, or a design doc.

## Purpose

This lens helps the reviewer judge whether an event-driven design *decouples what should be decoupled* and *designs its events to evolve*, and whether reaching for events at all is the right instrument for the communication at hand. Its job is to separate genuine decoupling — a producer that should not know or wait for its consumers, parts of the business that change for different reasons at different speeds, a fan-out to several independent reactions — from event machinery bolted onto a call that one synchronous, in-process invocation already handles correctly and more simply. The default bias is strongly toward the simplest mechanism that satisfies the requirement: **if a single in-process call with one consumer and no need for asynchrony does the job, a direct call is the correct design and an event is overengineering.** When communication genuinely crosses a boundary where the producer should be decoupled from its consumers, the lens gives the reviewer a consistent way to ask "what does this event mean, who depends on it, can its producer change without breaking them, and is the temporal/consistency coupling understood?" and to phrase the answer as a consequence-anchored finding rather than a style preference. It is equally a tool for restraint — for confirming that the *absence* of events (a plain synchronous call) is correct and that no comment is warranted.

## When to Use

Consult this lens when the design or change involves communicating across a boundary by producing or reacting to events, including:

- A component **publishes an event** (in-process via `ApplicationEventPublisher`, or across processes to a message broker) that other components react to, rather than calling them directly.
- A **domain event** raised inside one bounded context is about to cross a boundary and become an **integration event** consumed by another context or service — the moment a `./ddd.md` domain event acquires external consumers.
- A choice is being made between **event notification**, **event-carried state transfer**, or **event sourcing** as the way to communicate a change — or that choice is implicit and worth surfacing.
- A workflow is being wired as a chain of components reacting to each other's events (**choreography**) versus a central coordinator driving them (**orchestration**) — the *coupling/visibility* facet of that choice (the *failure/compensation* facet belongs to [`./saga.md`](./saga.md)).
- An **event schema** is being defined or changed, and consumers already depend on its shape (a field renamed, removed, retyped, or made required).
- A design relies on the **order** in which events arrive, or partitions a stream by some key.
- A design is becoming **event-driven by default** — events introduced for communication that has one consumer, needs an immediate answer, or is in-process and synchronous, where the events buy nothing and cost asynchrony and eventual consistency.

Do NOT engage this lens merely because the vocabulary appears. A class named `OrderEvent`, a package called `events`, an `@EventListener`, or a topic on a broker is not itself a reason to engage — the trigger is communication across a boundary where the *style* decision (decouple by events vs. call directly), the *event design* (notification vs. state transfer vs. sourcing), the *schema evolution*, or the *topology* (pub/sub vs. point-to-point, choreography vs. orchestration) has a consequence, not the words used to label it. **And do NOT engage merely because** something is published as an event: a single in-process event with one synchronous consumer is often just an indirected method call, and the right finding may be to call directly. **Do NOT engage** to litigate workflow compensation (that is [`./saga.md`](./saga.md)), a read/write model split (that is [`./cqrs.md`](./cqrs.md)), or the mechanics of tailing a DB log (that is [`./cdc.md`](./cdc.md)).

## Core Principle

Event-driven architecture is a tool, not an objective. Communicating by events means a producer announces that *something happened* and does not call, wait for, or in general know its consumers; consumers react independently, asynchronously, on their own schedule. That **inverts the dependency** (the producer no longer depends on the consumer) and **removes temporal coupling** (the consumer need not be available when the producer acts) — and in exchange it gives up the synchronous call's immediacy, its single transaction, its straight-line traceability, and its strong consistency. The style is justified only when it does concrete work a synchronous call cannot:

- **Decouples a producer from consumers that should evolve independently** — the producer emits the event and is done; consumers are added, removed, or changed without touching the producer, which is the whole point of the inversion.
- **Fans out one occurrence to several independent reactions** — one `OrderPlaced` drives shipping, invoicing, loyalty, and notification, each owned by a different part of the system, none of which the producer should call directly.
- **Removes temporal coupling** so a producer can proceed when a consumer is slow or down, the event waiting durably until the consumer catches up, instead of a synchronous call failing because a downstream is unavailable.
- **Communicates a business outcome across a context or service boundary** while letting each side own its own model and react on its own terms.

That bargain — looser coupling and availability bought with **eventual consistency**, **lost end-to-end traceability** (the flow exists nowhere in one place), **at-least-once delivery** (and therefore the idempotency burden on every consumer), and a **broker to operate** — must be made consciously, and it is never free. None of it is needed when the communication is one synchronous call to one consumer that needs an immediate, consistent answer.

**If a single synchronous in-process call with one consumer and no need for asynchrony suffices, communicating by events is overengineering.** This is one of the most common correct findings for this lens. An event there adds indirection (control flow vanishes from the call site), eventual consistency where strong consistency was free, an at-least-once/idempotency requirement, and — across processes — a broker to run and monitor; spending all of that on a call a plain method invocation already makes synchronous, atomic, and traceable is pure cost. This connects directly to [`./ddd.md`](./ddd.md): a *domain event* may be a perfectly good in-context decoupling, and it becomes an *integration event* — a published contract with external consumers — only when it crosses a boundary; that crossing, and the coupling and schema obligations it creates, is what this lens owns. This lens leans hard against dogma: **"no comment" — and "this doesn't need to be event-driven" — are valid, frequent, and correct outcomes**, and recommending *against* unnecessary event machinery is itself a finding, not silence.

## Severity Calibration

Apply these four levels to event-driven findings (see [`../rules/severity-rubric.md`](../rules/severity-rubric.md) for the shared definitions):

- **MUST** — A style, design, or evolution decision causes a concrete defect or serious hazard now: an **event-carried-state-transfer event whose schema is changed in a breaking way with no versioning or compatibility strategy** (a producer renames, removes, retypes, or makes-required a field, and every consumer deserializing that event breaks silently the moment the new producer deploys — a broken contract across the boundary); **wiring events across a boundary whose parts are not actually independent** — using events as the coupling mechanism for work that must stay strongly consistent (e.g. a single aggregate's invariant), so the design pays the broker tax for zero decoupling and can persist a state that should be impossible (a distributed-monolith hazard; whether that invariant truly belongs inside one aggregate is [`./ddd.md`](./ddd.md)'s judgment — this lens owns that *events are the wrong coupling here*); **depending on an ordering the broker does not guarantee for correctness** (a consumer that assumes `OrderUpdated` always arrives after `OrderCreated`, on a topology where reordering or per-partition interleaving is possible, so it processes an update for an order it has not seen and corrupts or drops state); a **breaking change to an integration event treated as an internal refactor** (the producer's team changes the event shape unaware that other services consume it, breaking them on deploy). These must be raised and must explain the failure mode.
- **SHOULD** — No defect today, but a specific change would clearly improve coupling, evolvability, or comprehension, and you can state the concrete benefit: a **chatty event-notification design** where a thin "something changed, call me back to find out what" event triggers N synchronous callbacks from each consumer to the producer for the data, when carrying the state in the event would remove the callbacks and the temporal coupling they reintroduce; an **evolving event with no schema/contract registry or compatibility check**, so the next change is one careless edit away from a silent break even though nothing is broken yet; an **unacknowledged temporal coupling** (a "fire-and-forget" event the producer in fact needs the consumer to have processed before it proceeds, so the asynchrony is a latent ordering bug waiting for load); a choreography whose cross-event flow is implicit and would be far more traceable made explicit before it grows another step (the *coupling/visibility* angle — defer the *failure-recovery* angle to [`./saga.md`](./saga.md)). Raise it when the benefit is real and nameable.
- **NIT** — The name of an event or a topic, the package an event type lives in, or an optional structural preference with no behavioral consequence (calling an event `OrderPlacedEvent` vs `OrderPlaced`). Worth a brief note, never a blocker.
- **NO_COMMENT** — The suggestion would be pure taste or speculative architecture: **"make this event-driven"** for an in-process call with **one consumer and zero need for asynchrony** (the synchronous call is simpler, atomic, and traceable — recommend *against* EDA, which is a finding, not silence); "use pub/sub instead of a direct call" / "publish an event here" / "this should go through the broker" where the communication is synchronous, single-consumer, and needs an immediate consistent answer; "use event sourcing" where a current-state table is honest and sufficient; choosing between two defensible topologies (point-to-point vs. pub/sub, notification vs. state transfer) where neither causes a named problem. Stay silent. **"This doesn't need to be event-driven" is among the most common correct outcomes of this lens.**

**Overriding rule:** every finding must name a concrete consequence — what breaks, what gets left inconsistent, what consumer silently fails to deserialize, what state corrupts under reordering, what coupling forces a coordinated change, or what concretely improves. "This should be event-driven" / "use pub/sub" / "this isn't really an event" is **not** a finding. "This `OrderPlaced` event is consumed by Shipping and Billing, and the producer's change drops the `currency` field with no versioning, so both consumers deserialize a null currency and compute the wrong totals the moment the new producer deploys" **is**. If no concrete consequence can be articulated, the finding is NO_COMMENT, no matter how far the design diverges from a textbook event-driven shape. Style (formatting, naming-case, import order, `this.`, whitespace) belongs to linters, never to review.

## Review Questions

Before raising or accepting an event-driven design (or its absence), the reviewer should be able to answer:

- Does this communication genuinely need to be event-driven — a producer that should not know or wait for its consumers, a fan-out to independent reactions, temporal decoupling — or is it one synchronous call to one consumer that needs an immediate, consistent answer? If the latter, why is there an event at all?
- What *kind* of event is this — a **notification** ("something happened, ask me for details"), **event-carried state transfer** ("something happened, here is the state you need"), or an **event-sourcing** record ("this is the fact, the state is derived from the log")? Is that the right kind for how consumers use it, and is the trade-off (staleness, callback chatter, coupling to the producer's data) understood?
- Is this a **domain event** staying inside one context, or an **integration event** crossing a boundary to external consumers? If it crosses, it is a *published contract* — who consumes it, and what breaks if its shape changes?
- How does this event's **schema evolve**? Can the producer add, rename, remove, retype, or require a field without breaking existing consumers — and is there a compatibility rule (e.g. additive-only / a schema registry) that makes the next change safe rather than lucky?
- What is the **coupling** between producer and consumers — does the producer know its consumers (it should not, in pub/sub) — and is there an **unacknowledged temporal coupling** where the producer actually depends on a consumer having processed the event before it proceeds?
- Is the coordination **choreography** (components react to each other's events, no coordinator) or **orchestration** (a coordinator drives them)? Can a maintainer still trace the end-to-end flow and find where it broke? (The *failure/compensation* side of this is [`./saga.md`](./saga.md)'s; here it is coupling and visibility.)
- Does the design rely on **event ordering or partitioning** that the broker actually guarantees, or does correctness assume an order the topology can violate?
- Is delivery **at-least-once**, and is every consumer therefore idempotent? (The decision that at-least-once is the contract is here; the durable idempotency *mechanism* is [`./spring-production-readiness.md`](./spring-production-readiness.md)'s and [`./saga.md`](./saga.md)'s.)
- Is this **pub/sub** (one event, many independent subscribers) or **point-to-point** (one event, one logical handler / a work queue)? Does the topology match how many consumers there are and whether they are independent?
- Are events being used **everywhere** — for synchronous, single-consumer, in-process calls too — so the system is a *distributed monolith*: tightly coupled parts connected through a broker, paying the cost of asynchrony with none of the independence?

## Heuristics

### Event Notification vs Event-Carried State Transfer vs Event Sourcing

**What to look for:** *What an event carries* and how consumers get the data they need from it. Three shapes recur. **Event notification** is a thin signal — `OrderPlaced { orderId }` — that says only *something happened*; a consumer that needs detail calls back to the producer to fetch it. **Event-carried state transfer** puts the state the consumers need *in* the event — `OrderPlaced { orderId, customerId, lineItems, total, currency }` — so a consumer can react without calling back. **Event sourcing** makes the event the *source of truth*: the system stores an append-only log of events and derives current state by replaying them, rather than storing current state and emitting a notification about it. The signal worth acting on is a mismatch between the shape chosen and how consumers actually use it: thin notifications that every consumer answers with a synchronous callback to the producer (the chatter and temporal coupling are back), or fat state-transfer events that couple every consumer to the producer's internal data shape, or event sourcing adopted where a current-state table was all anyone needed.

**Why it matters:** The three trade differently and the wrong choice has a concrete cost. **Notification** keeps the event tiny and the producer's data private, but if many consumers need the detail they all call back synchronously — reintroducing the temporal coupling and the fan-in load the event was supposed to remove, and making the producer a hot dependency again. **State transfer** removes those callbacks (the consumer has what it needs) and lets a consumer work even when the producer is down, but it **couples every consumer to the producer's event schema** and ships data that can be **stale** by the time it is read, and a careless schema change now breaks all of them (see Schema Evolution below). **Event sourcing** gives a perfect audit log, time-travel, and the ability to build new read models by replay — but it is a *large* commitment: every state change is an event, you need snapshotting, replay, and versioning of historical event shapes forever, and most systems that reach for it needed a current-state table and an audit log, not a rebuilt persistence model. The concrete consequence of getting this wrong is either a chatty, tightly-temporally-coupled system (over-thin notifications), a fragile fan-out that breaks on every producer change (un-versioned state transfer), or a hugely over-built persistence layer (event sourcing where none was warranted).

**When NOT to comment:** When the shape fits the use — a notification that consumers genuinely only need as a trigger (they have their own copy of the data or only need to know it happened), or state transfer where the carried fields are stable and consumers need them and the staleness is acceptable — there is nothing to flag. Choosing notification vs. state transfer is frequently a defensible judgment call with no named consequence either way; that is NO_COMMENT. And do **not** propose event sourcing as an improvement — it is almost never the lighter option, and "use event sourcing" with no named problem is NO_COMMENT.

**Modern Java/Spring idiom:** Model the event as an immutable `record` carrying exactly the fields its contract promises (verify the project's Java version supports records). For notification, keep it to identifiers; for state transfer, include the state consumers need and treat that set as a versioned contract. Publish in-context via `ApplicationEventPublisher`; across services, serialize to a broker with an explicit, versioned schema (see Schema Evolution). For event sourcing specifically, verify whether the project actually has an event store and the appetite for replay/snapshotting before treating it as an option at all — never assume an event-sourcing framework is present, and prefer a current-state table plus (if needed) an audit/outbox for the common case.

**Key review questions:** What does this event carry, and is that the right amount — does every consumer answer a thin notification with a synchronous callback (chatter), or does a fat event couple consumers to the producer's data and ship staleness? Is this event sourcing, and if so, does the system genuinely need a derived-from-log model, or did it need a current-state table and an audit trail?

**Example review comment:** "SHOULD: `OrderPlaced` carries only `orderId`, and both Shipping and Billing immediately call back to Ordering to fetch the line items and total. That's two synchronous fan-in calls per order back to the producer on every placement — the temporal coupling and the load the event was meant to remove are back. Carrying the line items, total, and currency in the event (event-carried state transfer) lets both consumers react without calling back, at the cost of coupling them to the event's schema — which means versioning it deliberately (see the schema-evolution concern). Weigh the callback chatter against that schema obligation."

### When NOT to be Event-Driven (the Synchronous-Call Default)

**What to look for:** An event introduced for communication that does not need it: a single in-process call to **one** consumer that needs an **immediate, consistent** answer, modeled as a publish-and-listen instead of a direct method call; a request/response interaction (the caller needs the result to continue) forced through an event and a callback; a within-one-transaction effect that must be atomic, emitted as an event that a listener handles outside the transaction. The tell is an event whose only consumer is one component, in the same process, that the producer effectively waits for anyway.

**Why it matters:** Communicating by events is not free, and applied to a synchronous single-consumer call it is pure cost: control flow disappears from the call site (a reader can no longer see what happens next), strong consistency becomes eventual where it was free, the at-least-once contract imposes idempotency the direct call never needed, and — across processes — you take on a broker to run and monitor. Worse, when the producer in fact needs the consumer to have finished (it is request/response in disguise), the asynchrony is an outright bug source: an *unacknowledged temporal coupling* that works in tests and tears under load when the consumer hasn't caught up. The concrete consequence is a system that is harder to read, weaker in its consistency, and more operationally heavy than the one-line method call it replaced — with no decoupling to show for it, because there is only one consumer and it is not independent.

**When NOT to comment:** When the communication genuinely needs events — the producer should not know or wait for its consumers, there is a fan-out to independent reactions, or temporal decoupling is the point — the event is correct and demanding a direct call would re-couple things that should evolve apart. The synchronous-call default is *not* a blanket preference against events; it is a default for the *single-consumer, immediate-answer, in-process* case. Do not invoke it against a real fan-out or a real cross-service decoupling.

**Modern Java/Spring idiom:** For a single synchronous, required, in-process effect, a plain method call (or a thin domain service) is the idiomatic and correct shape — visible, atomic with the caller's transaction, and trivially traceable. Reach for `ApplicationEventPublisher` / `@EventListener` only when there are genuinely independent reactions or the producer should not depend on the consumer. Where an effect must be atomic with the originating work, a synchronous in-transaction listener or a direct call is right; an `@TransactionalEventListener(AFTER_COMMIT)` runs outside the transaction and cannot make the effect atomic (this is the [`./ddd.md`](./ddd.md) domain-events caveat and the [`./spring-production-readiness.md`](./spring-production-readiness.md) transaction-boundary concern — defer the runtime mechanics there).

**Key review questions:** How many consumers does this event have, and are they independent of the producer? Does the producer need an immediate answer or need the consumer to have finished before it proceeds — i.e. is this request/response wearing an event's clothes? Is this in-process and synchronous already, so an event adds asynchrony and eventual consistency for nothing?

**Example review comment:** "Recommend AGAINST events here (NO_COMMENT on EDA, flagging the simpler design): `ValidateOrder` publishes a `ValidationRequested` event that exactly one in-process listener handles, and `placeOrder` then waits for a `ValidationCompleted` event before continuing. That's a synchronous request/response between two components in the same process, expressed as two events and a callback — control flow is now invisible at the call site, the effect is no longer atomic with the order transaction, and you've taken on at-least-once semantics for a call that needs an immediate answer. A direct `validator.validate(order)` method call is simpler, atomic, and traceable. Keep the event only if validation actually fans out to independent reactions you didn't show."

### Domain Event vs Integration Event (Crossing the Boundary)

**What to look for:** An event that starts as a **domain event** — raised inside one bounded context to decouple a producer from in-context reactions — and is about to be **consumed by another context or service**, becoming an **integration event**. The signals: an event type imported by code in a different context's package; an in-process `ApplicationEvent` being republished to a broker; a `./ddd.md` domain event whose listeners now live outside the context that raised it. The key thing to recognize is the *moment of crossing*, because a domain event and an integration event have very different obligations.

**Why it matters:** A domain event inside one context is an *internal* affair — its producer and consumers are owned by the same team, deploy together, and can change the event's shape in one commit. An integration event is a **published contract**: it has consumers the producer does not own and does not deploy with, so changing its shape is changing an API that other services depend on. Treating an integration event like an internal one — refactoring its fields freely — breaks those consumers silently on the next deploy (see Schema Evolution). Conversely, exposing a *domain* event's full internal shape across a boundary leaks the producer's internal model to its consumers, so an internal modeling change now ripples outward (this is the anti-corruption / context-mapping concern that [`./ddd.md`](./ddd.md) owns at the boundary; here the consequence is specifically that the event becomes a coupling channel). The concrete consequence of conflating the two is either a published contract changed as if it were private (breaking external consumers) or the producer's internal model leaking outward through the event (coupling contexts that should be independent).

**When NOT to comment:** When an event stays entirely within one context — one team, one deployable, consumers and producer changing together — the published-contract obligations do not apply, and demanding schema-registry ceremony for an in-process domain event is overhead. The finding exists at and beyond the *crossing*: when the event has, or is about to have, consumers the producer does not own. Do not treat every domain event as an integration event preemptively.

**Modern Java/Spring idiom:** Keep domain events as in-process `ApplicationEvent`s consumed within the context. When a domain outcome must reach another context, translate it deliberately into an explicit *integration event* with its own versioned schema — do not republish the internal domain event's shape wholesale; map it to a published-contract DTO at the edge, the same anti-corruption discipline [`./ddd.md`](./ddd.md) describes for context boundaries. The reliable mechanism for getting that integration event out *exactly when the state change committed* is the transactional outbox (and a relay, often CDC) — that machinery is owned by [`./saga.md`](./saga.md) and [`./cdc.md`](./cdc.md); cross-reference, do not re-derive it here.

**Key review questions:** Is this event staying inside one context, or does it (now or soon) have consumers in another context or service? If it crosses, is it being treated as a published contract with a versioned schema, or as an internal type the producer can refactor freely? Does the event expose the producer's internal model across the boundary?

**Example review comment:** "MUST: `OrderStateChanged` is an internal domain event in Ordering, but this change subscribes the Shipping service to it directly over the broker. It is now an integration event — a published contract Shipping depends on — yet it carries Ordering's full internal state shape and has no version. The next time Ordering refactors that internal model, Shipping breaks on deploy with no warning. Define an explicit integration event with its own versioned schema, mapped from the domain event at the edge (the anti-corruption translation the DDD lens describes), and version it as an API. Getting it published atomically with the state change is the outbox concern in the saga/cdc lenses."

### Producer/Consumer Coupling and Temporal Coupling

**What to look for:** *What the producer knows about its consumers* and *whether the producer secretly depends on a consumer having processed the event*. Two distinct couplings. **Structural coupling:** in true pub/sub the producer publishes to a topic and does not know who (if anyone) subscribes — watch for a "publisher" that actually enumerates its consumers, calls them by name, or whose behavior branches on which consumers exist (that is a synchronous call list in disguise). **Temporal coupling:** the producer publishes "fire and forget" but in fact needs the consumer to have *finished* before it proceeds or before another action is valid — watch for a producer that publishes an event and then immediately reads state the consumer is supposed to have written, or a second operation that is only correct after the first event was handled.

**Why it matters:** The entire benefit of the event-driven style is the inverted dependency: the producer is free of its consumers, so consumers can be added, removed, and changed without touching it, and the producer can proceed when a consumer is slow or down. If the producer *knows* its consumers (structural coupling), that benefit is gone — adding a consumer means editing the producer, and you have a synchronous call list wearing pub/sub clothing, with all the cost of a broker and none of the decoupling. If the producer *depends on the consumer having finished* (temporal coupling) but communicates asynchronously, you have a latent ordering bug: it passes every test where the consumer happens to be fast, and under load — when the consumer lags — the producer reads state that isn't there yet, or performs a second action that the unfinished first one invalidates, and the result is wrong with no error. The concrete consequence is either no decoupling despite the broker (structural) or silent, load-dependent corruption (temporal).

**When NOT to comment:** When the producer genuinely does not know its consumers and does not depend on their having processed the event — the normal, correct pub/sub shape — there is nothing to flag. When a temporal dependency is *acknowledged and handled* (the producer waits for an explicit acknowledgement, or the second action is itself event-driven off the consumer's result), the coupling is designed-for, not a bug. Do not invent a temporal coupling that the design has already addressed.

**Modern Java/Spring idiom:** Publish to a topic (or via `ApplicationEventPublisher`, which already decouples the publisher from listeners in-process) without the producer naming its consumers; add a consumer by subscribing, never by editing the producer. Where a producer truly needs a consumer's result to continue, make that continuation itself event-driven (react to the consumer's *result* event) rather than publishing-then-reading-shared-state, or — if an immediate answer is genuinely required — recognize that this is request/response and a synchronous call is the honest shape (see "When NOT to be Event-Driven"). Verify the broker's actual delivery and ordering semantics rather than assuming the producer and consumer stay in step.

**Key review questions:** Does the producer know or enumerate its consumers — if so, what is the broker buying over a direct call list? After publishing, does the producer (or a closely following action) depend on a consumer having already processed the event? If a consumer lags under load, does anything the producer does next become wrong?

**Example review comment:** "MUST: `confirmOrder` publishes `OrderConfirmed` and, on the next line, reads the `Shipment` that the Shipping consumer is supposed to create in reaction. That's a temporal coupling expressed as fire-and-forget: in tests the consumer is fast and the shipment is there, but under load it lags and `confirmOrder` reads a missing shipment and proceeds as if there were none — silently wrong, no error. Either make the step that needs the shipment react to a `ShipmentCreated` event (keep it event-driven and ordered by the events), or, if confirm genuinely needs the shipment immediately, this is request/response and a synchronous call is the honest design."

### Event Schema Evolution and Compatibility

**What to look for:** A change to the *shape* of an event that consumers already depend on, and whether anything makes that change safe. The signals: a field **renamed, removed, retyped, or newly made required** on an event type that is serialized and consumed elsewhere (especially an integration event across services); the absence of any **versioning, schema registry, or compatibility rule** on an event whose shape is going to keep changing; a producer's team editing an event as if it were a private internal type when it is in fact a published contract. The strongest signal of a hazard is event-carried state transfer (consumers depend on the carried fields) combined with a breaking schema edit and no compatibility strategy.

**Why it matters:** An event consumers deserialize is a **contract**, and a breaking change to it breaks them — but, unlike a synchronous API where a broken call fails loudly at the call site, a broken *event* contract fails **silently and asynchronously**: the producer deploys, starts emitting the new shape, and every consumer's deserializer either throws on a missing/required field, reads a null where a value used to be, or mis-binds a retyped field — and because consumers run independently, the failures surface as scattered, delayed errors (or worse, silently wrong data) across services, far from the producer's deploy that caused them. A rename or removal with no compatibility strategy is therefore not a refactor; it is a **broken cross-boundary contract** that takes down or corrupts every consumer the moment the new producer ships. This is the single most damaging event-driven defect because it is invisible at the producer's review: the producer's tests pass, its build is green, and the break lives in other teams' services. The concrete consequence is silent, system-wide consumer breakage on a deploy that looked safe.

**When NOT to comment:** When the change is **backward-compatible** — adding an optional field, widening a type a consumer reads leniently, a new event type alongside the old — existing consumers are unaffected and there is nothing to flag. When the event stays entirely within one context (producer and all consumers deploy together, schema changed in one commit), the published-contract obligations do not apply — do not demand a schema registry for an in-process domain event. And do not insist on a heavyweight registry for a small system with one team and one deployable where additive-only discipline already suffices.

**Modern Java/Spring idiom:** Make event evolution **additive-only** by default — add optional fields, never rename/remove/retype an in-use one; introduce a *new* event version (a `v2` type or a version field) when a breaking change is genuinely needed, and run old and new in parallel until consumers migrate. For cross-service events, a **schema registry** (e.g. an Avro/Protobuf/JSON-Schema registry with a configured compatibility mode — *verify the project actually runs one before recommending it; never assume a registry, Avro, or Protobuf is present*) turns "is this change safe?" into a build-time check instead of a production surprise. Configure consumers to **tolerate unknown fields** (e.g. Jackson `FAIL_ON_UNKNOWN_PROPERTIES = false`) so an additive producer change does not break them. Keep integration-event DTOs separate from the domain model so an internal change does not silently reshape the wire contract.

**Key review questions:** Is this event consumed by code the producer's team does not own and deploy with — i.e. is it a published contract? Is this change additive (safe) or breaking (rename/remove/retype/now-required)? If breaking, what makes it safe — a new version run in parallel, a registry compatibility check — or does every consumer break silently on the producer's next deploy?

**Example review comment:** "MUST: this renames `totalAmount` to `total` and drops `currency` on `OrderPlaced`, which Shipping and Billing both consume as event-carried state. There's no version and no registry, so the moment this producer deploys, both consumers deserialize a null `currency` and a missing `total` and compute wrong figures — silently, asynchronously, in services whose tests still pass. Either keep `totalAmount`/`currency` and *add* the new fields (additive-only), or publish an `OrderPlaced` v2 alongside v1 and migrate consumers before retiring v1. Longer term, a schema-registry compatibility check would catch this at build time instead of in production — if the project runs one."

### Ordering, Partitioning, and Delivery Guarantees

**What to look for:** A consumer whose **correctness depends on the order** in which events arrive, and whether the topology actually guarantees that order. The signals: a handler that assumes a "created" event always precedes an "updated"/"deleted" event for the same entity; logic that breaks if two events for the same key are processed out of order or concurrently; a stream **partitioned** by a key (so order is guaranteed only *within* a partition, not across) where the code assumes global order; or no partitioning thought given at all where per-entity order matters. Also watch for the **delivery guarantee** assumption — code that assumes exactly-once delivery, or that ignores at-least-once and is therefore not idempotent.

**Why it matters:** Most brokers guarantee ordering only within a partition (and only when a single consumer reads it), and many guarantee **at-least-once**, not exactly-once, delivery. Code that assumes global ordering, or that an "update" can never overtake a "create," will — on a partitioned or multi-consumer topology, or after a redelivery — process events out of the order it expects: it applies an update to an entity it hasn't created (drop or error), processes a delete before the create (a resurrected or orphaned record), or double-applies a redelivered event. These failures are intermittent and load- and topology-dependent: they pass single-threaded, single-partition tests and corrupt state in production. The concrete consequence is silently corrupted or dropped state when reality delivers events in an order the broker never promised. The fix for *ordering* is usually to **partition by the entity key** (so all events for one entity land in one ordered partition) and/or to make handlers tolerant of reordering (version/sequence checks); the fix for *at-least-once* is **idempotency** — and that idempotency *mechanism* is owned by [`./spring-production-readiness.md`](./spring-production-readiness.md) and [`./saga.md`](./saga.md), so this lens names the *requirement* and cross-references the *how*.

**When NOT to comment:** When order does not matter (events are commutative — order-independent — or each is self-contained), no ordering guarantee is needed and demanding partitioning is ceremony. When the design already partitions by the entity key and reads each partition with one consumer (so per-entity order holds), or handlers already carry a version/sequence check that tolerates reordering, the concern is handled. Do not propose strict global ordering — it is expensive and usually unnecessary; per-key ordering plus idempotency is the normal correct shape.

**Modern Java/Spring idiom:** Partition by the entity/business key so all events for one entity are ordered within one partition; keep one consumer per partition where per-key order must hold. Make handlers tolerant of reordering with a version or sequence number on the event (process only if newer; ignore stale). Treat delivery as at-least-once and make every consumer idempotent (the durable dedup *mechanism* — a processed-id table, a unique constraint, an idempotent upsert — is the production-readiness/saga concern; cross-reference it). *Verify the actual broker and its ordering/delivery semantics before relying on them — never assume exactly-once delivery or global ordering.*

**Key review questions:** Does this consumer's correctness depend on event order? If so, what guarantees that order — is the stream partitioned by the entity key with a single consumer per partition, or is the code assuming a global order the broker doesn't provide? Is delivery at-least-once, and is the handler idempotent for the redelivery that implies?

**Example review comment:** "MUST: the `AccountBalance` consumer applies each `BalanceChanged` event by overwriting the balance, and it assumes events arrive in emission order. The topic is partitioned by region, not by account, so two changes to the same account can land in different partitions and be processed out of order — the older value wins and the balance is silently wrong. Partition by `accountId` so all of one account's events are ordered in one partition, and/or carry a sequence/version on the event and apply only if newer. (Delivery is also at-least-once here, so the handler needs to be idempotent — the dedup mechanism is the production-readiness lens's concern.)"

### Pub/Sub vs Point-to-Point and Choreography vs Orchestration (Topology)

**What to look for:** The *shape of the wiring*. **Pub/sub vs point-to-point:** is one event delivered to *many independent subscribers* (pub/sub — Shipping, Billing, and Loyalty each react to `OrderPlaced`), or to *one logical handler* / a work queue where each message is processed once by one of a pool of workers (point-to-point)? Watch for pub/sub used where there is really one consumer (a topic with a single subscriber that could be a queue), or point-to-point used where several independent reactions are forced through one handler. **Choreography vs orchestration:** is a multi-step flow wired as components reacting to each other's events with no coordinator (choreography), or driven by a central coordinator (orchestration)? Here the facet to judge is **coupling and visibility** — can a maintainer trace the end-to-end flow? — *not* failure recovery and compensation, which is [`./saga.md`](./saga.md)'s.

**Why it matters:** The topology decides how the system couples and how traceable it is. **Pub/sub** is right for a genuine fan-out to independent consumers — the producer publishes once, consumers come and go without touching it. **Point-to-point** is right for work distribution (one logical handler, possibly load-balanced across workers) where each message must be handled exactly once by one consumer; using pub/sub there invites accidental double-processing if more than one subscriber appears, and using point-to-point for a real fan-out forces independent reactions to share a handler and recouples them. For **choreography vs orchestration**, the coupling/visibility consequence is concrete: choreography is simple and decentralized for a few steps, but as steps multiply the end-to-end flow exists *nowhere explicitly* — it is an emergent property of who-listens-to-what, so "why did this never finish?" means reconstructing an event chain across services; orchestration centralizes the flow in one traceable place at the cost of a coordinator that can accrete logic. The wrong choice yields either a flow nobody can follow (over-grown choreography) or a coordinator that owns too much. (This lens shares the choreography/orchestration topic with [`./saga.md`](./saga.md) **by design**: this lens frames the *architectural coupling and visibility* call — how coupled are the parts, can you trace the flow; the saga lens frames it for *failure and compensation* — what happens to consistency when a step fails. Cross-reference; do **not** re-derive compensation here.)

**When NOT to comment:** A two- or three-step choreography among a couple of components is perfectly fine — demanding an orchestrator for it adds a coordinator to maintain for a flow that is already traceable. A small orchestrator that just sequences a few reactions is fine; do not call it a "god coordinator" until it actually absorbs business rules. Pub/sub vs point-to-point is often a defensible call with no named consequence; choosing either is NO_COMMENT unless the mismatch causes a concrete problem (accidental double-processing, recoupled reactions, an untraceable flow).

**Modern Java/Spring idiom:** Pub/sub in-process via `ApplicationEventPublisher` with multiple `@EventListener`s, or across services via a broker topic with multiple subscriber groups; point-to-point via a single queue/consumer group where one worker handles each message. Choreography via each component owning its reaction to others' events; orchestration via an explicit coordinator `@Service` (or a state machine) that sequences the steps — but the coordinator's *failure handling, compensation, and stuck-flow recovery* belong to [`./saga.md`](./saga.md); here, only judge whether the topology keeps the flow traceable and the parts appropriately coupled. *Verify the broker and any messaging library are actually present before recommending broker-specific topology; never assume a broker is on the classpath.*

**Key review questions:** Is this a fan-out to independent consumers (pub/sub) or work distribution to one logical handler (point-to-point), and does the wiring match? For a multi-step flow: is it choreography or orchestration, how many steps, and can a maintainer trace the end-to-end path today — or is the flow emergent and untraceable? (If the question is "what happens to consistency when a step fails," that's the saga lens, not this one.)

**Example review comment:** "SHOULD: the fulfilment flow is choreographed across five events (`OrderPlaced` → `PaymentCaptured` → `ShipmentReserved` → `InvoiceIssued` → `Notified`), each in a different service reacting to the previous one. From a coupling and *visibility* standpoint, the end-to-end sequence is written down nowhere — diagnosing 'placed but never shipped' means reconstructing the chain across five services' logs, and a sixth step would make it worse. Before that step lands, consider an explicit coordinator that sequences the flow in one traceable place; weigh it against the coordinator you'd then maintain. (How that flow stays *consistent when a step fails* — compensation, stuck-flow recovery — is the saga lens's call, not this one's.)"

## Anti-Patterns

- **Event-driven for a single synchronous in-process call** — *Diff/design:* a publish-and-listen (or a request `Event` plus a `Completed` callback `Event`) used for one in-process consumer that the producer needs an immediate answer from. *Harm:* control flow vanishes from the call site, strong consistency becomes eventual where it was free, an at-least-once/idempotency burden appears on a call that needs an immediate result, and (across processes) a broker must be run — all with **no decoupling**, because there is one consumer and it is not independent. *Fix:* a direct synchronous method call (or a thin domain service); reach for events only for a genuine fan-out, a producer that should not know its consumers, or real temporal decoupling.

- **Distributed monolith via events** — *Diff/design:* events used to split a *single aggregate's invariant* (or a tightly-coupled, must-be-atomic effect) across services, so parts that always change together and need strong consistency communicate through a broker. *Harm:* the invariant can no longer be enforced atomically — the system can persist a state that should be impossible — while the design pays the full event-driven tax (asynchrony, eventual consistency, at-least-once, a broker) and gets **none** of the independence, because the "decoupled" services are in fact one unit. It is the worst of both worlds: distributed-system cost with monolith coupling. *Fix:* keep one aggregate's invariant inside one aggregate and one transaction (per [`./ddd.md`](./ddd.md)); use events only across boundaries whose parts genuinely change independently. If two services must be strongly consistent for an invariant, the boundary is wrong.

- **Breaking an event schema with no versioning (broken contract across the boundary)** — *Diff/design:* a field renamed, removed, retyped, or made required on an integration event consumers deserialize, with no version, no registry, and no compatibility rule. *Harm:* the moment the producer deploys, every consumer breaks **silently and asynchronously** — deserializers throw, read nulls, or mis-bind — in services whose own tests pass, far from the producer's green build. *Fix:* additive-only evolution by default; introduce a new event version run in parallel for a genuinely breaking change; a schema-registry compatibility check turns the break into a build-time error (verify a registry is actually present).

- **Chatty notification with a synchronous callback fan-in** — *Diff/design:* thin event-notification events whose every consumer immediately calls back synchronously to the producer to fetch the detail. *Harm:* the temporal coupling and producer-as-hot-dependency the event was meant to remove are back — N synchronous fan-in calls per event, and a consumer can no longer work when the producer is down. *Fix:* carry the state consumers need in the event (event-carried state transfer), accepting the schema-versioning obligation that creates; or, if the callbacks are cheap and rare, leave it — this is a SHOULD, not always a defect.

- **Unacknowledged temporal coupling (fire-and-forget that isn't)** — *Diff/design:* a producer publishes an event "fire and forget" and then (or in a closely following action) depends on the consumer having already processed it — reading state the consumer writes, or taking a step only valid after the consumer finished. *Harm:* a latent, load-dependent bug — correct in tests where the consumer is fast, silently wrong under load when the consumer lags (missing state read as absent, a second action invalidated by an unfinished first). *Fix:* make the dependent step react to the consumer's *result* event (keep it ordered by events), or recognize it as request/response and use a synchronous call.

- **Ordering assumed that the broker doesn't guarantee** — *Diff/design:* a consumer whose correctness assumes global or cross-partition event order (an "update" never overtakes a "create"), on a topology partitioned by the wrong key or read by multiple consumers. *Harm:* on a partitioned or multi-consumer topology, or after a redelivery, events process out of expected order — an update applied to a not-yet-created entity, a delete before a create, a stale value overwriting a newer one — silently corrupting or dropping state, intermittently and only under production conditions. *Fix:* partition by the entity key with one consumer per partition for per-entity order, and/or carry a version/sequence and apply only if newer; treat delivery as at-least-once and make handlers idempotent (the dedup mechanism is the production-readiness/saga concern).

- **Event sourcing where a current-state table would do** — *Diff/design:* the persistence model rebuilt as an append-only event log with replay, for a system that needed current state plus (at most) an audit trail. *Harm:* a large, permanent commitment — every state change is an event, historical event shapes must be versioned forever, snapshotting and replay must be built and maintained — paid for a benefit (time-travel, derived read models) the system does not need. *Fix:* a current-state table, plus an audit log or an outbox if change history or reliable publishing is the real need; reserve event sourcing for cases that genuinely require a derived-from-log model, and verify an event store and the replay appetite actually exist first.

## Modernization (Java/Spring idioms)

Treat these as the idiomatic modern expressions of event-driven concepts — but **inspect the target project first and never assume a version, a broker, a schema registry, a serialization library, or any messaging or streaming infrastructure is present.** Establish the Java language level, the Spring Boot version, the persistence stack, the messaging/streaming technology actually in use (if any), and which libraries are on the classpath before recommending anything. A suggestion the project cannot compile, or that assumes a broker, a registry, or a tool it does not have, is worse than no suggestion.

Frame every version- or dependency-specific claim as *verify against the actual project; never assume a broker, a registry, or a library is present.*

- **In-process events** — `ApplicationEventPublisher` to publish; `@EventListener` for synchronous, in-transaction reactions (atomic with the originating work); `@TransactionalEventListener(phase = AFTER_COMMIT)` for reactions that must run only after a durable commit. Note that an **AFTER_COMMIT listener runs after the originating transaction has committed, with no active transaction**, so its side effect is outside that transaction and cannot roll it back, and any DB write inside it is non-transactional by default and may be silently lost unless the listener opens its own transaction (`@Transactional(propagation = REQUIRES_NEW)`). Reserve AFTER_COMMIT for retry-safe, external-ish effects — the same caveat [`./ddd.md`](./ddd.md) and [`./spring-production-readiness.md`](./spring-production-readiness.md) state; defer the runtime mechanics there. *Verify the Spring version supports the listener you suggest.*
- **Cross-service events via a broker** — participants publish to and react from a message broker. This is where at-least-once delivery, idempotent consumers, schema versioning, and partitioning/ordering all become live concerns. *Verify a broker is actually present and confirm its delivery and ordering semantics; never assume one is on the classpath, and never assume exactly-once delivery or global ordering.*
- **Events as immutable records** — model the event payload as an immutable `record` carrying exactly its contract's fields (identifiers for notification; the needed state for state transfer). *Verify the project is on Java 16+ (records GA) and isn't standardized on a different DTO approach.*
- **Schema evolution and registries** — additive-only evolution by default; a new event version run in parallel for a breaking change; configure consumers to tolerate unknown fields (e.g. Jackson `FAIL_ON_UNKNOWN_PROPERTIES = false`). A **schema registry** with a compatibility mode (Avro/Protobuf/JSON-Schema) turns a breaking change into a build-time failure instead of a production surprise. *Verify the project actually runs a registry and which serialization format it uses before recommending any of them — never assume a registry, Avro, or Protobuf is present.*
- **Reliable publishing (the dual-write boundary)** — when a state change must reliably produce an event, the **transactional outbox** (write the event in the same local transaction as the state change, relay it asynchronously) is the mechanism, often relayed by **CDC**. This is the *decision* to publish reliably; the outbox/relay *machinery* is owned by [`./saga.md`](./saga.md) and [`./cdc.md`](./cdc.md). *Verify whether the project already has an outbox, a broker, and a CDC pipeline before proposing any.*
- **Idempotent consumers** — because delivery is at-least-once, every state-changing consumer must be idempotent. This lens names the *requirement*; the durable *mechanism* (a processed-id table, a unique constraint, an idempotent upsert) is owned by [`./spring-production-readiness.md`](./spring-production-readiness.md) and [`./saga.md`](./saga.md). *Verify the broker's actual delivery guarantee rather than assuming exactly-once.*
- **Orchestration vs choreography wiring** — choreography via each component's own `@EventListener`/broker subscription; orchestration via an explicit coordinator `@Service` or a state machine. The coordinator's *failure handling, compensation, and stuck-flow recovery* belong to [`./saga.md`](./saga.md); here, judge only coupling and traceability. *If a state-machine library is suggested, verify it is actually a dependency; never assume one is present.*
- **Read/write model separation** — if the design separates a write model from event-derived read models, that read/write split is [`./cqrs.md`](./cqrs.md)'s subject; cross-reference it rather than judging the read-model design here.

When a single synchronous in-process call to one consumer does the job, say so plainly — the broker, the topic, and the event are not the goal; communication decoupled to exactly the degree the problem actually requires is.

## Suggested Comment Style

Keep comments respectful, consequence-first, and severity-honest. Lead with what concretely breaks, stays inconsistent, or recouples (or what improves), not with a style label — name the failure mode so the author can weigh the trade-off rather than feel judged for not following a textbook. State severity honestly; a NIT should read like a NIT, not a blocker. Offer the alternative, and **acknowledge the trade-off the event-driven style makes** — eventual consistency, lost traceability, the schema-versioning obligation, the broker to operate. When a synchronous call would do, say so directly and recommend *against* events; that is a finding, not silence.

Example openers:

- "If this producer changes the event's shape, what happens to the consumers that deserialize it?" (asks the schema-evolution question)
- "After publishing this, does anything depend on the consumer having already processed it?" (names the temporal-coupling hazard)
- "How many consumers does this event have, and are they independent of the producer?" (probes whether EDA is buying anything)
- "This looks like one synchronous call to one consumer — does it need to be an event at all?" (recommends restraint)
- "Does this consumer's correctness depend on an order the broker actually guarantees?" (asks the ordering question)
- "NIT (not a blocker): ..." (flags taste-level, optional)
- "This fan-out reads cleanly — the producer doesn't know its consumers and each reaction is independent. No change needed." (explicitly endorses)

Example comments (neutral-domain):

- "MUST: this renames `totalAmount` to `total` on `OrderPlaced`, which Shipping and Billing consume as carried state, with no version and no registry. The moment this producer deploys, both consumers deserialize a missing field and compute wrong totals — silently, in services whose own tests pass. Keep the old field and *add* the new one (additive-only), or publish a v2 alongside v1 and migrate consumers first."
- "MUST: `OrderConfirmed` is published fire-and-forget, but the next line reads the `Shipment` the consumer is meant to create. That's a temporal coupling: fine in tests where the consumer is fast, silently wrong under load when it lags. Make the dependent step react to a `ShipmentCreated` event, or — if it needs the shipment immediately — use a synchronous call; this is request/response, not a fan-out."
- "MUST: splitting the `Order`-total invariant across the Ordering and Pricing services via events means the rule can't be enforced atomically — the system can persist an order whose total doesn't match its lines, with no error. These two parts always change together and need strong consistency; keep the invariant in one aggregate and one transaction (per the DDD lens). Events here buy the cost of a broker with none of the decoupling."
- "Recommend AGAINST an event here: `validateOrder` publishes a request event and waits for a completion event, but there's exactly one in-process consumer and `placeOrder` needs the result to continue. A direct method call is simpler, atomic with the transaction, and traceable. Keep the event only if validation actually fans out to independent reactions."
- "SHOULD: the fulfilment flow is choreographed across five events with the sequence written down nowhere, so a failed run takes five services' logs to trace. Before a sixth step lands, an explicit coordinator would make the flow traceable in one place — weigh it against the coordinator you'd maintain. (How it stays consistent when a step *fails* is the saga lens's concern, not this one's.)"

## Integration (architecture-review and spec-author)

The consuming skills use this lens as a judgment aid, with their own intent — `architecture-review` evaluatively at system/design altitude (is the event-driven style decoupling what should be decoupled and designing its events to evolve?), `spec-author` generatively (should this communication be event-driven, what kind of event, and how is its schema versioned?). In both:

- **Never comment because events are absent.** A design that communicates by a synchronous call, or keeps a flow in one transaction, is not a finding by itself. Absence is worth raising only when you can name the concrete consequence (a producer forced to know consumers that should be independent, a fan-out hand-coded as a call list that recouples on every new reaction). And recommending *against* unnecessary event machinery — "a synchronous call is simpler, atomic, and traceable here" — is a legitimate, frequent finding, not silence.
- **Comment only when there is a real, articulable consequence** — a consumer that silently fails to deserialize a changed event, a distributed monolith whose invariant can't be enforced, state corrupted by an ordering the broker never promised, a temporal coupling that tears under load, a callback fan-in that recouples a producer, or a flow nobody can trace.
- **Never block on topology preference.** "Use pub/sub" / "publish an event" / "use orchestration instead of choreography" / "use event sourcing" without a named consequence is NIT at most, and usually NO_COMMENT.
- **Always tag findings** `MUST` / `SHOULD` / `NIT` per [`../rules/severity-rubric.md`](../rules/severity-rubric.md) so the author can triage by severity. (NO_COMMENT is the silent fourth outcome and, for this lens, a common one — frequently in the form "this doesn't need to be event-driven.")
- **A few strong findings beat many weak ones.** One well-justified MUST about an un-versioned breaking event schema, a distributed monolith, or a broker-unsupported ordering assumption is worth more than a list of "consider pub/sub" observations — drop the weak ones to NO_COMMENT rather than padding the review.
- **Stay in your lane — this lens owns the *style*, not its neighbors' machinery.** This lens reasons about the decision to be event-driven, event design (notification vs. state transfer vs. sourcing), domain-vs-integration events, producer/consumer and temporal coupling, schema evolution, ordering/partitioning, delivery-guarantee *requirements*, and topology (pub/sub vs. point-to-point, the *coupling/visibility* facet of choreography vs. orchestration) — *system/design altitude*. Defer the rest:
  - **Workflow consistency under failure — compensation, retriable-vs-compensatable steps, the pivot, saga state and stuck-flow recovery, the *failure* facet of choreography/orchestration** → [`./saga.md`](./saga.md). This lens frames choreography/orchestration as *how coupled and how traceable*; the saga lens frames it as *what happens to consistency when a step fails*. Cross-reference; never re-derive compensation here.
  - **Aggregate and consistency boundary, one-aggregate-per-transaction, where a domain event lives, anti-corruption at a context boundary** → [`./ddd.md`](./ddd.md). A *domain event* is DDD's; the moment it crosses a boundary to external consumers and becomes an *integration event* — its coupling and schema obligations — is this lens's.
  - **Read/write model separation, materialized read models** → [`./cqrs.md`](./cqrs.md).
  - **Mechanics of capturing a DB change as a stream, log tailing, the outbox-relay implementation** → [`./cdc.md`](./cdc.md).
  - **Runtime defect of a single dual-write/listener/consumer in a diff — the *durable idempotency mechanism*, the transaction-boundary self-invocation, the unbounded consumer** → [`./spring-production-readiness.md`](./spring-production-readiness.md). This lens owns the *architectural decision* (event-carried state vs. notification, schema versioning, at-least-once as the contract); that lens owns the line-level runtime mechanics.
  Defer to those rather than re-litigating their concerns here.
