# CDC Lens

Reusable Change Data Capture knowledge for Java/Spring: when capturing committed row-level changes from a datastore and streaming them to downstream consumers actually serves the system, and whether a CDC pipeline — and capturing raw tables versus an intentional outbox — is the right instrument at all. **The intent of using this lens comes from the consuming skill, not from the lens itself.**

## How skills use this lens

This lens has two consumers, used with opposite intents. **`architecture-review`** reads it **evaluatively**, at **system/design altitude** — whether a change in one datastore genuinely must propagate to another system, where the capture point sits (a raw business table or an intentional outbox), what contract that capture exposes, how ordering and duplicates are handled downstream, and whether the read model or index the stream feeds can silently fall behind — not line-by-line PR nitpicking. The review-flavored sections below (severity calibration, comment style, integration contract) are written for that design-review intent: *does a row-level change here genuinely need to reach another system as a stream, and if so is the capture point an intentional, stable contract — or is the design coupling consumers to an internal schema, dropping deletes, double-applying changes, and standing up a connector and a broker for work an app-emitted event would have done?*

That shared use — evaluative for `architecture-review`, generative for `spec-author` (which reads this lens to *decide* how a committed change should propagate and whether a capture pipeline is warranted when writing a spec, rather than to judge it after the fact) — is exactly why this knowledge lives in `lenses/` rather than inside one skill: each consumer supplies its own intent, the lens supplies the knowledge. It stays focused on the concrete decisions both intents can act on, not over-generalized. A consuming skill loads it only when the design or change actually involves capturing and streaming datastore changes as described in "When to Use" below — never just because the words "CDC," "Debezium," or "Kafka" appear in a name, a package, or a design doc.

## Purpose

This lens helps the reviewer judge whether streaming committed changes out of a datastore actually serves the system, and whether a CDC pipeline — at all, and over raw tables versus an outbox — is the right instrument for the propagation the design needs. Its job is to separate a genuine capture-and-stream requirement — a read model, a search index, a cache, or a warehouse that must track a source store the consumer does not own; a reliable outbox relay; bulk replication — from a CDC pipeline bolted onto work that you control end to end, where an intentional domain event would propagate the change with far less coupling and far less operational weight. The default bias is strongly toward the simplest propagation that satisfies the requirement: **if you own the source application and can emit an intentional event (or write an outbox row) when the change happens, capturing raw tables and streaming row diffs is coupling and infrastructure you may not need.** When you genuinely do not control the source, or you need the outbox relayed reliably, or you are replicating in bulk, the lens gives the reviewer a consistent way to ask "what is captured, what contract does that expose, how do deletes and duplicates and ordering behave, and what watches the lag?" and to phrase the answer as a consequence-anchored finding rather than a tooling preference. It is equally a tool for restraint — for confirming that a CDC pipeline is *not* warranted, that an intentional event or an outbox is the simpler design, and that no comment beyond that recommendation is needed.

## When to Use

Consult this lens when the design or change involves capturing committed changes from a datastore and propagating them as a stream, including:

- A change committed in **one datastore must propagate to another system** — a CQRS read model, a search index, a cache, a data warehouse, or another service's store — and that other system has to track the source as it changes.
- An application needs **reliable event publishing** and the design relays a **transactional outbox** by tailing it with CDC, so an intentional event becomes durable exactly when the state change commits and is shipped at-least-once.
- Data must be **replicated or synchronized without modifying the source application** — a legacy or third-party store whose changes you must observe, where adding publish code to the source is not an option.
- You **do not control the source application** that writes the store, so emitting an intentional event at the write site is impossible and observing the store's changes is the only way to learn of them.

Do NOT engage this lens merely because the vocabulary appears. A connector configuration, a package called `cdc`, a topic named `order-changes`, or a `Debezium`/`Kafka` dependency on the classpath is not itself a reason to engage — the trigger is a real requirement to capture a datastore's committed changes and stream them to a consumer that must track them, not the words used to label it. **And do NOT engage merely because** events are wanted between parts of the system you control: when you own the source application and a state change should trigger downstream work, an **intentional domain event** (or a write to an outbox the source emits deliberately) is the propagation mechanism — capturing the raw tables underneath it with a CDC pipeline adds a connector, a broker, and a schema-coupling problem to do what the application could have published directly.

## Core Principle

CDC is a tool, not an objective. It captures **committed** row-level changes — inserts, updates, deletes — from a datastore and turns them into an **ordered stream of change events** that downstream consumers apply. It learns of those changes after they commit: by reading the database **transaction log** (the WAL / binlog / redo log) once the change is durable, by reading a change table, or by polling a column. The fact that a design matches the CDC shape is never, by itself, a reason to adopt it.

The single most important design choice this lens exists to surface is **what gets captured**:

- Capturing **raw business tables** turns your internal schema into a **de-facto public integration contract**. Every consumer now couples to your column names, types, and table layout, and any schema refactor — a rename, a split, a type change, a normalization — ripples outward to every consumer, often **silently**, because the row diff says nothing about intent. The capture point is the contract, and you did not choose it deliberately; the database did.
- Capturing an **outbox table** inverts this. The application writes **intentional, stable event rows** to an outbox in the **same local transaction** as the state change (the dual-write / outbox mechanism from [`./saga.md`](./saga.md)), and CDC merely **tails the outbox and ships those intentional events reliably**. The contract is the event the application chose to publish, not the raw row, so the internal schema can be refactored freely behind it.

A CDC pipeline earns its keep only when it does concrete work a simpler propagation cannot:

- **You do not control the source** application or store, so you cannot add publish code at the write site, and observing the store's committed changes is the only way to learn of them.
- **The outbox relay** — CDC tailing an outbox the source deliberately writes is a robust way to publish intentional events at-least-once without a polling relay, durable exactly when the state change committed.
- **Bulk replication or sync** — copying a whole store to a warehouse, a replica, or a read store, where a snapshot plus a continuous change stream is the right shape.

**If you own the source application and an intentional domain event — or an outbox relayed by CDC — suffices, a CDC pipeline over raw tables is coupling and operational weight you may not need.** This is the most common correct finding for this lens. A CDC pipeline is heavy infrastructure: a connector to configure and operate, a broker to run, a snapshot to bootstrap, ordering and idempotency to get right, and lag to monitor — and capturing raw tables adds a schema-coupling liability on top. Spending all of that to move a change between parts of the system you control, when the application could have published an intentional event directly, is pure cost. This connects directly to [`./saga.md`](./saga.md) (CDC over an outbox is one way to run the outbox relay) and to [`./cqrs.md`](./cqrs.md) (CDC is one way to keep a separate read model in sync) — in both, CDC is *a* mechanism, not the goal. This lens leans hard against dogma: **"no comment" — and "this doesn't need a CDC pipeline; emit an intentional event instead" — are valid, frequent, and correct outcomes**, and recommending an outbox over raw-table capture, or an app-emitted event over a connector, is itself a finding, not silence.

## Severity Calibration

Apply these four levels to CDC findings (see [`../rules/severity-rubric.md`](../rules/severity-rubric.md) for the shared definitions):

- **MUST** — A capture or delivery decision causes a concrete defect or serious hazard now: a non-idempotent consumer applying an **at-least-once** change stream, so a redelivered change is applied twice and the read model double-counts a total or an action double-fires, silently; **query/polling-based** capture used where **deletes or intermediate states matter**, so deleted rows are never propagated (orphaned read-model rows served as live data) and changes between polls are lost with no error; **per-key ordering not preserved**, so two updates to the same `Order` are applied out of order and the projection settles on a stale value; a **snapshot-to-stream handover** that loses or duplicates changes at the boundary, so the read model starts life already wrong; **raw business tables captured as an integration contract** with no abstraction, so a column rename or table refactor breaks every consumer with no compile-time or runtime warning; the read model or search index fed by CDC with **no lag or backpressure monitoring**, so it silently falls behind and serves stale or wrong data while everything looks healthy. These must be raised and must explain the failure mode.
- **SHOULD** — No defect today, but a specific change would clearly improve correctness-under-redelivery, decoupling, or operability, and you can state the concrete benefit: a CDC pipeline over raw tables where relaying an **outbox** the source already could write would decouple consumers from the schema before the next refactor breaks them; **trigger-based** capture adding write-path overhead where **log-based** capture would be lower-impact and capture deletes the triggers miss; a snapshot/stream pipeline whose **schema-evolution** behavior is undefined, so the first source DDL change will surprise the consumers; a lag metric that exists but is **unbounded and unalerted**, so a stall is recoverable only once a user notices. Raise it when the benefit is real and nameable.
- **NIT** — Naming of a connector or topic, the package a relay lives in, or an optional structural preference with no behavioral consequence (calling a topic `order-events` vs `orders.changelog`). Worth a brief note, never a blocker.
- **NO_COMMENT** — The suggestion would be pure taste or speculative infrastructure: "add CDC" / "stream this with Debezium" / "publish these tables to Kafka to be ready" when you control the source and an intentional event or a same-store read would do, or when both capture mechanisms are defensible and neither causes a named problem. Stay silent. **"This doesn't need a CDC pipeline" is the single most common correct outcome of this lens** — and recommending *against* an unnecessary pipeline, or *toward* an outbox over raw-table capture, is itself a finding, not silence.

**Overriding rule:** every finding must name a concrete consequence — what breaks, what gets applied twice, what is silently dropped, what goes stale unnoticed, what couples to a schema and breaks on a refactor, or what concretely improves. "Use CDC" / "add Debezium" / "this should stream through Kafka" is **not** a finding. "This consumer applies the `Order` change stream by incrementing a running total, and the stream is at-least-once, so a redelivered change increments twice and the reported revenue silently drifts above the real figure" **is**. If no concrete consequence can be articulated, the finding is NO_COMMENT, no matter how far the design diverges from a textbook CDC shape. Style (formatting, naming-case, import order, `this.`, whitespace) belongs to linters, never to review.

## Review Questions

Before raising or accepting a CDC pipeline (or its absence), the reviewer should be able to answer:

- Does this change genuinely need to leave its datastore and reach another system, and do you **control the source application**? If you own the source, why is a CDC pipeline capturing raw tables doing work an intentional domain event (or an outbox) would do with less coupling?
- **What is captured** — raw business tables, or an intentional **outbox** the application writes? If raw tables, is the internal schema now a public contract every consumer couples to, and what happens to them on the next refactor?
- Which **capture mechanism** is used — log-based (reads the transaction log), trigger-based (write-path triggers), or query/polling (a timestamp/version column)? Does that mechanism capture **deletes** and **intermediate states**, and what does it cost the source?
- Is the stream **at-least-once**? If so, what makes each consumer **idempotent** — a dedup by change/business id, or a naturally idempotent upsert — so a redelivered change applies once?
- Is **ordering preserved per key** — are all changes for one entity routed to the same partition via the **entity key**, so two updates to the same row apply in commit order? Is cross-key global ordering being relied on (it is not guaranteed)?
- Does the pipeline do an initial **snapshot** then switch to **streaming the log**, and is the **handover** clean — no change lost or duplicated between the consistent point-in-time copy and the start of the stream?
- How do **schema changes** in the source (DDL, a renamed or added column) flow through the stream, and how do consumers cope — do they break, ignore, or adapt?
- Are **deletes** handled by consumers, or are deleted source rows left as orphaned read-model/index rows served as live data? (And in log-compacted topics, are tombstones understood?)
- Is **replication lag** monitored and bounded, with **backpressure** handled, so the read model or index cannot silently fall arbitrarily behind and serve stale data with no alarm?
- If there is no CDC pipeline, is the absence correct — does an intentional event, an outbox, or a same-store read serve the propagation without a named problem — or is a consumer that does not own the source genuinely unable to learn of changes any other way?

## Heuristics

### Capture Mechanism: Log-based vs Trigger-based vs Query/Polling

**What to look for:** How committed changes are captured from the source store. **Log-based** capture reads the database **transaction log** (the WAL / binlog / redo log) after a change commits — it sees inserts, updates, and **deletes**, in commit order, with low impact on the source write path. **Trigger-based** capture installs triggers that write a change row on every insert/update/delete — it captures the same events but on the **source's write path**, adding overhead to every write. **Query/polling-based** capture periodically reads a timestamp or version column and emits rows changed since the last poll. The signal worth acting on is a mechanism whose limitations collide with what the design needs — most often polling chosen where deletes or every intermediate state matter.

**Why it matters:** The mechanisms differ in exactly the ways that cause silent data defects. **Polling cannot see deletes** — a deleted row simply stops appearing in the query, so a consumer never learns it is gone and leaves an orphaned row in the read model or index, served as if live. Polling also **misses intermediate states**: if a row changes three times between two polls, the consumer sees only the last value, so a projection or audit that depends on each transition is silently incomplete. And polling **lags by the poll interval** by construction. Triggers capture deletes and every change, but pay for it on the source write path — every business write now also writes a change row, raising latency and contention on the hot path the triggers fire on. Log-based capture avoids both: it reads the log after commit, so it captures deletes and every committed state in order without touching the write path. Where log-based capture is available it is generally the right default; the concrete consequence of the wrong mechanism is dropped deletes, lost intermediate states, or write-path overhead that the design did not intend.

**When NOT to comment:** When the source store and the available tooling genuinely do not support log-based capture and polling's limitations do not matter for this consumer (no deletes occur, only the latest value is needed, and the poll lag is acceptable), polling is a legitimate choice — do not demand log-based capture the platform cannot provide for a case its limits do not bite. When triggers are already in place, capture deletes, and the write-path overhead is measured and acceptable, do not push a migration for its own sake. The mechanism choice is frequently a defensible judgment with no named consequence either way — that is NO_COMMENT.

**Modern Java/Spring idiom:** Log-based capture via a connector that reads the source database's transaction log — Debezium (as an embedded engine or via Kafka Connect) is one such tool — streaming change events to consumers. Trigger-based and polling capture are sometimes the only options for a store whose log is inaccessible. *Verify what the source store and the project's actual tooling support before recommending a mechanism; never assume Debezium, a log reader, or a connector is present, and never assume the source database exposes its log.*

**Key review questions:** Does this mechanism capture deletes, and does this consumer need them? Does it capture every intermediate state, or only the latest value at each poll — and does that matter here? If it is trigger-based, what does the extra write cost the source's hot path? Is log-based capture available, and is there a reason it was not used?

**Example review comment:** "MUST: the search index is kept current by polling `Order` rows whose `updatedAt` changed since the last run. Polling can't see deletes — a cancelled `Order` is removed from the table and simply stops appearing in the query, so the index never learns it's gone and keeps serving the cancelled order as a live hit. It also collapses several updates between polls into one. If the source's transaction log is accessible, log-based capture would catch deletes and every committed change in order; otherwise the consumer needs an explicit delete signal. Verify what the store and the project's tooling actually support."

### Raw-Table Capture vs the Outbox

**What to look for:** **What the pipeline captures.** The trigger is a CDC stream sourced from **raw business tables** — `Order`, `Customer`, `Invoice` rows captured directly and shipped to consumers as row diffs — versus a stream sourced from an **outbox** table the application writes intentional event rows into. The strongest signal of a problem is consumers parsing raw table rows (column names, types, foreign keys) as their integration input, with no intentional event layer between the internal schema and the consumers.

**Why it matters:** Capturing raw tables makes your **internal database schema a public integration contract**, and you did not choose it deliberately — the table layout *is* the contract. Every consumer now couples to your column names and types, so any schema change you make for internal reasons — renaming a column, splitting a table, normalizing, changing a type — **ripples to every consumer, silently**, because a raw row diff carries no intent and no version. A change that should have been a private refactor becomes a breaking change to systems you may not even know consume the stream. The **outbox** inverts this: the application writes an **intentional, stable event** (`OrderPlaced`, `InvoiceIssued`) to an outbox table in the **same local transaction** as the state change — the dual-write/outbox mechanism in [`./saga.md`](./saga.md) — and CDC tails the *outbox*, shipping the events the application chose to publish. The contract is the intentional event, owned and versioned deliberately, so the internal schema can be refactored freely behind it. This is the same propagation that keeps a [`./cqrs.md`](./cqrs.md) read model in sync, and the same intentional-event-versus-raw-row distinction [`./ddd.md`](./ddd.md) draws between a published domain event and a raw state change. The concrete consequence of capturing raw tables as a contract is a codebase that cannot refactor its own schema without breaking unknown downstreams; the consequence of the outbox is that the schema and the published contract evolve independently.

**When NOT to comment:** When you **do not control the source** application and cannot add an outbox to it — a legacy or third-party store you must observe — capturing raw tables (often into your own anti-corruption translation, per [`./ddd.md`](./ddd.md)) is the only option, and demanding an outbox the source's owner will never write is not actionable. When the capture feeds a **bulk replica or warehouse** whose explicit job is to mirror the source schema (an analytics copy that *wants* the raw shape), raw-table capture is appropriate. When an outbox already fronts the stream and consumers read intentional events, the concern is met — do not pile on. The schema-coupling finding exists when you own the source, the consumers couple to raw rows, and a refactor would break them.

**Modern Java/Spring idiom:** The **outbox event router** pattern — the application writes an intentional event row to an `outbox` table in the same `@Transactional` method as the aggregate change, and a connector (Debezium has an outbox routing capability, for example) tails the outbox and publishes the event, rather than capturing the business tables. Keep the event a deliberately versioned contract. For a source you do not own, capture raw tables but translate them at the edge into domain types (an anti-corruption adapter, [`./ddd.md`](./ddd.md)) so the foreign schema does not leak inward. *Verify whether the project has an outbox, a connector with outbox routing, and a broker before proposing any of them; never assume Debezium, an outbox table, or Kafka is present.*

**Key review questions:** Is the stream sourced from raw business tables or from an intentional outbox? Do you control the source application — could it write an outbox instead? When the source schema is refactored, what happens to the consumers reading the stream — do they break silently, and would you even know who consumes it? Is the captured shape an intentional contract you version, or the internal table layout by accident?

**Example review comment:** "MUST: the read model and a downstream service both consume a CDC stream captured directly off the `Order` and `LineItem` tables, parsing the raw columns. That makes the internal schema a public contract — when we normalize `LineItem` or rename a column for our own reasons, every consumer breaks with no warning, because the row diff carries no intent. Since we own the source, write intentional `OrderPlaced`/`OrderUpdated` events to an outbox table in the same transaction as the `Order` change and capture the *outbox* instead (the outbox mechanics are in the saga lens). Consumers then couple to a versioned event, and we can refactor the schema freely. If this fed a warehouse that wants the raw shape, or if we didn't own the source, raw capture would be defensible — but here we do."

### Snapshot Then Stream

**What to look for:** How the pipeline **bootstraps** an existing store and then keeps current. A CDC setup over a non-empty source typically takes an **initial snapshot** — a consistent point-in-time copy of the existing rows — and then **switches to streaming** the transaction log from the snapshot's position onward. The trigger is any pipeline started against a store that already holds data, and specifically the **handover** between the snapshot and the start of the stream. The strongest signal of a problem is a handover with no defined consistency point — a snapshot taken at one moment and streaming started at another, with a gap or an overlap between them.

**Why it matters:** The read model or index has to start from the source's current contents, not just future changes, or it begins life missing everything written before the stream started. So the pipeline snapshots the existing rows, then streams changes from the **exact log position** the snapshot corresponds to. The hazard is at the **handover**: if streaming starts from a position *after* the snapshot's, changes that committed in the gap are **lost** — the consumer never sees them and the projection is permanently missing data it has no way to know about. If streaming starts from a position *before* the snapshot's, the same changes arrive **twice** — once baked into the snapshot, once in the stream — and a non-idempotent consumer double-applies them. Either way the read model **starts life already wrong**, and the error is invisible because nothing compares it back to the source. A correct handover establishes a single consistent boundary: every committed change is either in the snapshot or in the stream, exactly once. The concrete consequence of getting it wrong is a projection that is silently incomplete or double-counted from the first day, with no signal until the numbers are noticed to be off.

**When NOT to comment:** When the consumer is started against an **empty** source (or is explicitly designed to track only changes from now on and the historical rows are irrelevant), there is nothing to snapshot and the handover concern does not arise — do not demand snapshot machinery for a stream that legitimately starts at zero. When the connector handles the snapshot/stream boundary and consumers are idempotent (so a small overlap is harmless), the requirement is met — do not invent a handover bug a tested, idempotent pipeline does not have. The finding exists when an existing store is bootstrapped and the handover can lose or duplicate changes that a consumer cannot tolerate.

**Modern Java/Spring idiom:** Rely on a connector that performs a **consistent snapshot and a clean handover to log streaming** at a known position, rather than hand-rolling "copy the table then start tailing" as two unsynchronized steps. Keep consumers idempotent (next heuristic) so any deliberate snapshot/stream overlap is harmless rather than corrupting. Have a **re-snapshot / rebuild** path so a read model that drifts can be re-derived from the source — the same rebuild requirement [`./cqrs.md`](./cqrs.md) puts on a materialized read model. *Verify the connector's snapshot behavior and the source store's capabilities rather than assuming a clean handover.*

**Key review questions:** Is this pipeline bootstrapping an existing store, or starting from empty? If it snapshots, does streaming resume from exactly the snapshot's log position — is there a gap (lost changes) or an overlap (duplicates) at the handover? Are consumers idempotent so an overlap is harmless? Is there a way to re-snapshot and rebuild if the read model drifts?

**Example review comment:** "SHOULD: the design copies the existing `Customer` rows into the read store and then, separately, starts tailing the log 'from now.' Those two steps aren't tied to one consistent position — any `Customer` change committed between the copy finishing and the tail starting is lost, and the read store is permanently missing it with nothing to flag the gap. Use the connector's consistent-snapshot-then-stream handover so every change is either in the snapshot or in the stream exactly once, and keep the consumer idempotent so a small overlap is harmless. A documented re-snapshot path would also let you rebuild if it drifts."

### Delivery, Ordering, and Idempotency

**What to look for:** Whether each consumer is **idempotent** under at-least-once delivery, and whether changes for one entity are kept **in order**. The triggers are: a consumer that applies a change stream by doing something non-idempotent (incrementing a running total, appending, firing an external action) with no dedup; and a topology where changes for the same entity can be processed out of commit order (no per-key partitioning, or parallel consumers without a key affinity). The strongest signal of a problem is an at-least-once stream feeding a consumer that assumes each change arrives exactly once and in order.

**Why it matters:** CDC streams are typically **at-least-once** — a change whose acknowledgement is lost, or that is replayed after a connector restart or a snapshot/stream overlap, is **redelivered**. A non-idempotent consumer applies the duplicate as if new: a running total is incremented twice, an external action double-fires, a read-model row is double-written. The defect is **silent** — there is no error, just a wrong number or a doubled effect discovered later. Idempotency is therefore not optional; it is what the at-least-once contract requires (the same requirement [`./saga.md`](./saga.md) imposes on saga participants). Separately, ordering is typically guaranteed only **per key (per partition)**, not globally: if two updates to the same `Order` are routed to different partitions or processed by parallel consumers without key affinity, they can be applied **out of commit order**, and the projection settles on the **stale** value — the older update wins. The fix is to **partition by the entity key** so all changes for one row go to one partition and are processed in order. Relying on **global** ordering across keys is a mistake — it is not provided, and a design that assumes it will reorder changes across entities. The concrete consequence of getting either wrong is a read model that is silently double-counted (no idempotency) or settled on stale values (no per-key ordering).

**When NOT to comment:** When the consumer is **naturally idempotent** — an upsert keyed by the entity id that sets the row to the change's state, a one-way state transition — a redelivered change is harmless and an explicit dedup table is redundant; do not demand one. When the apply logic does not depend on order (each change carries the full current state and the latest wins by key) and per-key ordering is already preserved by partitioning, the ordering concern is met. When the stream genuinely offers stronger guarantees that are verified, do not design around a weakness it does not have. Flag idempotency and ordering only where a duplicate or a reorder would actually corrupt the consumer's state.

**Modern Java/Spring idiom:** Make the consumer **idempotent** — dedup by the change's source position / a business id recorded with the apply, or express the apply as an **upsert keyed by the entity id** (set-to-state, not add-to-state) so redelivery is a no-op. Partition the stream by the **entity key** (the `OrderId`, the `CustomerId`) so per-key ordering holds and parallel consumers keep one key on one partition. Do not rely on exactly-once delivery or global ordering from the stream. *Verify the actual stream's delivery and ordering guarantees and the partition key before relying on either; never assume exactly-once or global order.*

**Key review questions:** Under at-least-once delivery, what happens if this change is applied twice — is the apply naturally idempotent (an upsert by id) or does it add/append/fire each time? Are all changes for one entity routed to one partition by the entity key, so they apply in commit order? Is the design relying on global ordering across keys, which the stream does not guarantee?

**Example review comment:** "MUST: the revenue read model applies the `Invoice` change stream by adding each change's amount to a running total. The stream is at-least-once, so a redelivered change adds twice and the reported revenue silently drifts above the real figure, with no error. Either dedup by the change's source position (record it with the apply and skip repeats) or recompute the total as an upsert from the full current state keyed by `InvoiceId`, so redelivery is a no-op. Also confirm changes are partitioned by `InvoiceId` — otherwise two updates to one invoice can apply out of order and settle on the stale amount."

### Schema Evolution

**What to look for:** How **schema changes in the source** flow through the stream and how consumers cope. The trigger is any pipeline where the source store's structure will change over time — a new column, a renamed or dropped column, a type change, a table split — and specifically whether the design has defined what the stream emits when that happens and what consumers do with it. The strongest signal of a problem is a pipeline with **no schema-evolution story**: the first source DDL change is assumed to "just work," with no thought to how the change event shape changes or how consumers tolerate it.

**Why it matters:** A source schema is not frozen, and CDC ships changes shaped by that schema, so a **DDL change reshapes the stream**. When the capture is over **raw tables**, a renamed or dropped column changes the row diff, and a consumer parsing that column breaks — exactly the schema-as-contract coupling the outbox heuristic warns about, now triggered by a routine migration. Even with an **outbox**, the event's shape is a contract that can evolve, and a consumer reading an old shape must tolerate the new one (or be versioned alongside it). Either way, the design needs an answer to "the source added/renamed/changed a column — what does the stream emit, and does the consumer break, ignore the unknown field, or adapt?" Without that answer, the first migration after the pipeline ships is a silent breaking change to downstreams, discovered when a consumer starts dropping records or throwing on an unexpected shape. The concrete consequence is a pipeline that constrains the source's ability to evolve its own schema — or breaks consumers when it does anyway.

**When NOT to comment:** When the source schema is genuinely stable and not expected to change (a frozen legacy store, a fixed reference table), an elaborate schema-evolution story is ceremony — do not demand a registry for a schema that will never move. When the design already has a versioning or schema-registry approach and consumers tolerate additive changes, the concern is met. The finding exists when the source schema *will* change and the design has no defined behavior for how the stream and the consumers cope.

**Modern Java/Spring idiom:** Front the stream with **intentional, versioned events** (the outbox) so the contract evolves deliberately rather than tracking raw DDL; make consumers tolerant of **additive** changes (ignore unknown fields) and version the event when a change is breaking. Where a schema registry is part of the stack, it can enforce compatibility on the change-event shape. *Verify whether the project has a schema registry, an outbox, or a versioning convention before recommending one; never assume a registry or a particular serialization is present.*

**Key review questions:** Will the source schema change over its life, and what does the stream emit when it does — a reshaped row diff, a new event version, an additive field? Does a consumer break, ignore the unknown field, or adapt? Is the captured contract decoupled from raw DDL (an outbox), or does every migration ripple to consumers? Is there a versioning or compatibility strategy for the change-event shape?

**Example review comment:** "SHOULD: the pipeline captures `Customer` rows directly and there's no story for what happens when the schema changes — and it will, the roadmap splits the address fields out next quarter. When that migration lands, the row diff changes shape and the consumers parsing those columns break silently. Fronting the stream with versioned `Customer*` events from an outbox would decouple consumers from the raw DDL so the split is a private refactor; at minimum, define how consumers tolerate an additive or renamed column before the first migration ships. Not blocking today, but the next migration will surface it."

### Operability and Lag

**What to look for:** Whether **replication lag** is monitored and bounded, whether **backpressure** is handled, and whether a stalled or falling-behind pipeline is **surfaced**. The trigger is any read model, search index, cache, or replica fed by a CDC stream — and specifically whether anything watches how far behind the source it is. The strongest signal of a problem is a consumer that can fall arbitrarily behind (or stop entirely) with **no metric and no alarm**, while the data it serves looks live.

**Why it matters:** A CDC-fed read side is **eventually consistent** with the source, and the lag is not fixed: a slow consumer, a burst of source writes, a connector that stalls or dies, or downstream backpressure can make the read model fall **arbitrarily far behind** — or stop updating entirely — while continuing to **serve stale data as if current**. Because the stale rows look exactly like fresh ones, the staleness is **silent**: nothing surfaces that the index is an hour behind, or that the connector died overnight, until a user notices the data is wrong and files a ticket. This is the operational failure mode that distinguishes a CDC pipeline from a same-store read — a same-store projection cannot lag, but a streamed one can, and the design must treat lag as a first-class signal. The countermeasures are concrete: **measure the lag** (the time or log-position gap between the source's latest commit and the consumer's last-applied position), **bound and alert** on it so a stall is caught before users are, and **handle backpressure** so a slow consumer slows or buffers rather than silently dropping. The concrete consequence of no lag monitoring is wrong data served confidently with no alarm — the same "stale read taken as truth" hazard [`./cqrs.md`](./cqrs.md) raises, here caused by an unwatched pipeline rather than an unhandled read-your-own-writes path.

**When NOT to comment:** When the consumer tolerates large staleness by design and nothing reads it for fresh decisions (an overnight analytics warehouse, a `Report` no one cross-checks against live state), strict lag alerting is ceremony — do not demand second-level monitoring for a daily batch. When lag is already measured, bounded, and alerted and backpressure is handled, the concern is met. The finding exists when a stale or stalled read side would actually serve wrong data for a decision and nothing would notice.

**Modern Java/Spring idiom:** Export the consumer's **lag** (source-latest vs last-applied, by time or log position) as a metric (Micrometer / an observability pipeline) and **alert** when it exceeds the staleness the consumers can tolerate; surface a **dead or stalled connector** rather than letting it fail quietly; ensure the consumer applies **backpressure** (bounded buffering, slowing intake) instead of dropping changes under load. *Verify the metrics and alerting stack is actually configured before relying on it; never assume a monitoring pipeline is wired up.*

**Key review questions:** Is the lag between the source and this read model measured, bounded, and alerted — would a stall be caught before a user notices? What happens when the consumer can't keep up — backpressure, or silently dropped changes? Is there any read where stale CDC-fed data drives a wrong decision, and is that staleness window known? If the connector dies, what surfaces it?

**Example review comment:** "MUST: the product search index is fed by a CDC stream, and nothing measures how far behind the source it is. If the connector stalls or the consumer can't keep up, the index keeps serving stale results that look live — a cancelled or repriced `Order` shows the old state — with no alarm until a customer reports it. Export the lag (source-latest vs last-applied) as a metric and alert when it exceeds the freshness the index needs, and confirm the consumer applies backpressure rather than dropping changes under load. A same-store read couldn't lag like this; a streamed one can, so lag has to be a watched signal."

## Anti-Patterns

- **A CDC pipeline where you control the source and an intentional event would do** — *Diff/design:* a connector plus a broker stood up to capture raw tables and stream them to a consumer, for a propagation between parts of the system the team owns end to end, where the source application could simply publish an intentional domain event (or write an outbox) when the change happens. *Harm:* the design pays the full CDC tax — a connector to configure and operate, a broker to run, a snapshot to bootstrap, ordering and idempotency to get right, lag to monitor — plus a schema-coupling liability from capturing raw tables, all to move a change the application could have published directly. Operational weight and coupling for nothing. *Fix:* emit an intentional domain event from the source (see [`./ddd.md`](./ddd.md)), relayed reliably via an outbox if needed (see [`./saga.md`](./saga.md)); reserve a CDC pipeline for a source you don't control, the outbox relay itself, or bulk replication.

- **CDC over raw business tables as an integration contract** — *Diff/design:* consumers integrate by parsing a CDC stream captured directly off the internal business tables — column names, types, foreign keys — with no intentional event layer between the schema and the consumers, for a source the team owns. *Harm:* the internal schema becomes a de-facto public contract; any refactor — a column rename, a table split, a type change — ripples to every consumer silently, because the raw row diff carries no intent and no version, and the team cannot refactor its own schema without breaking downstreams it may not even know about. *Fix:* emit intentional, versioned events to an outbox table in the same transaction as the state change, and capture the *outbox* (the outbox event-router pattern), so consumers couple to a deliberate contract and the schema evolves freely behind it. For a source you don't own, translate the raw capture into domain types at the edge ([`./ddd.md`](./ddd.md)).

- **A non-idempotent consumer under at-least-once delivery** — *Diff/design:* a consumer applies the change stream by doing something non-idempotent (incrementing a total, appending, firing an external action) with no dedup, on a stream that delivers at-least-once. *Harm:* a redelivered change — after a connector restart, a lost ack, or a snapshot/stream overlap — is applied twice, so the read model double-counts or an action double-fires, silently, discovered only when the number or the effect is noticed to be wrong. *Fix:* make the consumer idempotent — dedup by the change's source position or a business id recorded with the apply, or express the apply as an upsert keyed by the entity id (set-to-state, not add-to-state) so redelivery is a no-op. Never rely on exactly-once delivery from the stream.

- **Query/polling capture where deletes or intermediate states matter** — *Diff/design:* a consumer that needs deletes or every committed transition is fed by polling a timestamp/version column. *Harm:* polling cannot see deletes (a deleted source row stops appearing and the consumer leaves an orphaned read-model/index row served as live data) and collapses several changes between polls into one (intermediate states silently lost), and it lags by the poll interval. *Fix:* use log-based capture (reads the transaction log, sees deletes and every committed change in order) where the store and tooling support it; if polling is the only option, ensure deletes are signalled explicitly and that only the latest value per row is actually needed.

- **No lag or backpressure monitoring on a CDC-fed read side** — *Diff/design:* a read model, search index, or cache fed by a CDC stream with nothing measuring how far behind the source it is and no handling for a consumer that can't keep up. *Harm:* the read side can fall arbitrarily behind — or stop entirely if the connector dies — while serving stale data that looks live; the staleness is silent and wrong data is served for decisions with no alarm until a user notices. *Fix:* export the lag (source-latest vs last-applied) as a metric and alert when it exceeds the tolerated freshness; surface a stalled or dead connector; apply backpressure (bounded buffering, slowed intake) rather than dropping changes under load.

- **Per-key ordering not preserved** — *Diff/design:* changes for the same entity can be processed out of commit order — no partitioning by the entity key, or parallel consumers without key affinity — and the apply depends on order. *Harm:* two updates to the same `Order` are applied out of order and the projection settles on the older, stale value; the read model disagrees with the source and there is no error. *Fix:* partition the stream by the entity key so all changes for one row go to one partition and apply in commit order; keep one key on one consumer. Do not rely on global ordering across keys — the stream does not provide it.

## Modernization (Java/Spring idioms)

Treat these as the idiomatic modern expressions of CDC concepts — but **inspect the target project first and never assume a connector, a broker, a CDC tool, or a particular source-store capability is present.** Establish the Java language level, the Spring Boot version, the persistence stack, whether the source store exposes a transaction log at all, and the messaging/streaming technology actually in use (if any) before recommending anything. A suggestion the project cannot run, or that assumes a connector or a broker it does not have, is worse than no suggestion.

Frame every version- or dependency-specific claim as *verify against the actual project; never assume Debezium, Kafka, a connector, or a broker is present.*

- **Log-based capture via a connector** — read the source database's transaction log (WAL / binlog / redo) after commit, capturing inserts, updates, and deletes in order with low source impact. **Debezium** is one such tool, runnable as an **embedded engine** inside a Spring application or via **Kafka Connect** streaming to a broker. *Verify the source store exposes a log the connector can read, and that Debezium / the connector / the broker are actually present; never assume any of them.*
- **The outbox event router for intentional events** — write an intentional, versioned event row to an `outbox` table in the **same local transaction** as the aggregate change, and have the connector tail the *outbox* (some connectors, e.g. Debezium, offer an outbox routing capability) rather than the business tables — so consumers couple to a deliberate contract, not the internal schema. This is the [`./saga.md`](./saga.md) **outbox relay** run by CDC. *Verify whether the project has an outbox table, outbox routing, and a broker before proposing them.*
- **Idempotent consumers** — dedup by the change's source position or a business/change id recorded with the apply, or express the apply as an upsert keyed by the entity id, because the stream delivers at-least-once. *Verify the stream's actual delivery guarantee rather than assuming exactly-once.*
- **Partition by entity key for per-key ordering** — route all changes for one entity to the same partition via the entity key, so updates to one row apply in commit order and parallel consumers keep one key on one partition; do not rely on global cross-key ordering. *Verify the partition key and the stream's ordering guarantee.*
- **Monitor lag** — export the consumer's lag (source-latest vs last-applied, by time or log position) as a metric (Micrometer / an observability pipeline) and alert when it exceeds the tolerated freshness; surface a stalled or dead connector. *Verify the metrics and alerting stack is configured before relying on it.*

Note the explicit connections to the sibling lenses: CDC is one way to run the **outbox relay** described in [`./saga.md`](./saga.md), and one way to keep a separate **read model** in sync as described in [`./cqrs.md`](./cqrs.md). In both, CDC is a mechanism, not the goal.

When you own the source and an intentional domain event (or an outbox) does the job, say so plainly — a CDC pipeline is not the goal; the change propagated to the systems that need it with the least coupling and the least operational weight the requirement actually demands is. Capturing an outbox is the right answer far more often than capturing raw tables, and emitting an intentional event is the right answer far more often than standing up a pipeline at all.

## Suggested Comment Style

Keep comments respectful, consequence-first, and severity-honest. Lead with what concretely breaks, double-applies, goes stale unnoticed, or couples to a schema (or what improves), not with a tooling label — name the failure mode so the author can weigh the trade-off rather than feel judged for not following a pattern. State severity honestly; a NIT should read like a NIT, not a blocker. Offer the alternative, and **acknowledge the trade-off** — the operational weight a connector and a broker add, the schema coupling raw capture creates, the eventual consistency and lag a stream introduces. When you own the source and an intentional event would do, say so directly and recommend *against* a CDC pipeline; that is a finding, not silence.

Example openers:

- "Do you control this source — could it just publish an intentional event instead of capturing its tables?" (recommends restraint)
- "This stream is at-least-once, so if a change is redelivered..." (names the duplicate hazard)
- "Capturing the raw tables makes the schema a public contract — when you rename a column..." (names the coupling)
- "Polling can't see deletes, so a deleted row..." (names the dropped-delete hazard)
- "Nothing measures how far behind the source this index is, so if it stalls..." (names the silent-staleness hazard)
- "NIT (not a blocker): ..." (flags taste-level, optional)
- "You don't own this source and need its changes — CDC is the right mechanism here, and the outbox isn't an option. No change needed." (explicitly endorses)

Example comments (neutral-domain):

- "I don't think this needs a CDC pipeline. We own the source application, and the only consumer is our own read model — the source can publish an intentional `OrderPlaced` event (relayed via an outbox if it must be reliable) instead of standing up a connector and a broker to capture the raw `Order` tables. That avoids the operational weight and the schema coupling for a propagation we fully control. Reserve CDC for a source we don't own, the outbox relay itself, or bulk replication."
- "MUST: consumers parse this CDC stream straight off the `Order` and `LineItem` tables, so the internal schema is now a public contract — a column rename or a table split breaks every consumer silently. Since we own the source, write intentional versioned events to an outbox in the same transaction and capture the outbox instead; consumers then couple to a deliberate contract and we can refactor freely. (Outbox mechanics: saga lens.)"
- "MUST: the read model adds each `Invoice` change's amount to a running total, but the stream is at-least-once — a redelivered change adds twice and revenue silently drifts high. Dedup by the change's source position, or recompute as an upsert from the full current state keyed by `InvoiceId` so redelivery is a no-op."
- "MUST: the search index is fed by polling `updatedAt`, which can't see deletes — a cancelled `Order` just stops appearing in the query, so the index keeps serving it as live. Log-based capture would catch deletes and every committed change in order if the store's log is accessible; otherwise the consumer needs an explicit delete signal."
- "MUST: nothing watches the lag on this CDC-fed index, so if the connector stalls it serves stale data that looks live with no alarm. Export source-latest vs last-applied as a metric and alert on it, and confirm the consumer applies backpressure rather than dropping changes."

## Integration (architecture-review)

The consuming skill uses this lens as a judgment aid at system/design altitude, not a checklist to enforce:

- **Never comment because CDC is absent.** A design that propagates a change with an intentional domain event, an outbox, or a same-store read rather than a CDC pipeline is not a finding by itself — when you own the source, that is usually the correct design. Absence is worth raising only when you can name the concrete consequence (a consumer that does not own the source and genuinely cannot learn of changes any other way). And recommending *against* an unnecessary CDC pipeline — "you own the source; emit an intentional event instead" — or *toward* an outbox over raw-table capture, is a legitimate, frequent finding, not silence.
- **Comment only when there is a real, articulable consequence** — a duplicate change double-applied under at-least-once, a deleted row dropped by polling, an out-of-order apply settling on stale state, a raw-table capture that breaks consumers on a refactor, a read side that silently falls behind, a snapshot/stream handover that loses or duplicates changes.
- **Never block on preference.** "Add CDC" / "stream this with Debezium" / "publish these tables to be ready" without a named consequence is NIT at most, and usually NO_COMMENT. Speculative streaming infrastructure with no current driver is NO_COMMENT.
- **Always tag findings** `MUST` / `SHOULD` / `NIT` per [`../rules/severity-rubric.md`](../rules/severity-rubric.md) so the author can triage by severity. (NO_COMMENT is the silent fourth outcome and, for this lens, the most common one — frequently in the form "this doesn't need a CDC pipeline; emit an intentional event instead.")
- **A few strong findings beat many weak ones.** One well-justified MUST about a non-idempotent consumer, a raw-table contract, or an unmonitored lag is worth more than a list of "consider CDC" observations — drop the weak ones to NO_COMMENT rather than padding the review.
- This lens reasons about how committed datastore changes are captured and streamed, what contract the capture point exposes, and how consumers cope with delivery, ordering, deletes, schema change, and lag — *system/design altitude*. The intentional domain event vs raw row-change distinction and aggregate modeling belong to [`./ddd.md`](./ddd.md); the transactional outbox, dual-write, at-least-once, and idempotency *mechanics* belong to [`./saga.md`](./saga.md); whether a separate read model should exist and its ownership/rebuild belong to [`./cqrs.md`](./cqrs.md); pattern-level structural calls belong to [`./design-patterns.md`](./design-patterns.md); test quality belongs to [`./testing.md`](./testing.md). Defer to those rather than re-litigating their concerns here.
