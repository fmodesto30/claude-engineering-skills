# Saga Lens

Reusable Saga-pattern knowledge for Java/Spring: when a unit of work must hold consistency across more than one aggregate, datastore, or service without a distributed ACID transaction — and when reaching for a saga at all is heavy machinery the problem does not justify. **The intent of using this lens comes from the consuming skill, not from the lens itself.**

## How skills use this lens

This lens has two consumers, used with opposite intents. **`architecture-review`** reads it **evaluatively**, at **system/design altitude** — where a workflow's consistency boundary falls, what happens to that workflow when a step in the middle fails, whether duplicate delivery doubles an effect, and how a half-finished process is observed and recovered — not line-by-line PR nitpicking. The review-flavored sections below (severity calibration, comment style, integration contract) are written for that design-review intent: *is consistency across these boundaries maintained correctly when steps fail and messages duplicate, or is the design either assuming an atomicity it cannot have, or paying for saga machinery a single local transaction would make unnecessary?*

That shared use — evaluative for `architecture-review`, generative for `spec-author` (which reads this lens to *decide* whether a saga is warranted and to design its compensation, idempotency, and outbox when writing a spec, rather than to judge them after the fact) — is exactly why this knowledge lives in `lenses/` rather than inside one skill: each consumer supplies its own intent, the lens supplies the knowledge. It stays focused on the concrete decisions both intents can act on, not over-generalized. A consuming skill loads it only when the design or change actually involves a multi-step, cross-boundary unit of work as described in "When to Use" below — never just because the word "saga," "orchestrator," or "compensation" appears in a name, a package, or a design doc.

## Purpose

This lens helps the reviewer judge whether a cross-boundary workflow keeps the system consistent under failure and duplication, and whether a saga is the right instrument for that workflow at all. Its job is to separate genuine distributed-consistency hazards — a step that can fail with no way to undo what came before, an event published outside the transaction that wrote the state, a participant that doubles its effect on a redelivered message — from saga ceremony bolted onto work that one local ACID transaction already makes atomic. The default bias is strongly toward the simplest mechanism that satisfies the requirement: **if the whole unit of work fits inside one aggregate and one datastore, one local transaction is the correct design and a saga is overengineering.** When the work genuinely spans aggregates, services, or stores and cannot be one transaction, the lens gives the reviewer a consistent way to ask "where is the consistency boundary, what happens on partial failure, and is each effect safe under at-least-once delivery?" and to phrase the answer as a consequence-anchored finding rather than a pattern preference. It is equally a tool for restraint — for confirming that the *absence* of a saga is correct and that no comment is warranted.

## When to Use

Consult this lens when the design or change involves a unit of work that genuinely crosses a consistency boundary, including:

- A single business outcome must update **more than one aggregate, datastore, or service** and cannot be committed as one local transaction (the work spans the Ordering store, the Payment store, and the Shipping store, for example).
- A **cross-service workflow with several steps that can each fail independently** — place the `Order`, capture the `Payment`, reserve the `Shipment`, send the `Notification` — where a failure partway through leaves earlier steps already committed.
- A **long-running process** whose steps are separated by waits, external callbacks, or human action, so the work cannot be held open inside one transaction.
- An **event chain that propagates a business outcome across contexts** — one context's commit triggers work in another, which triggers a third — and the end-to-end consistency of that chain is what the design is asserting.
- A design that **publishes a message or event in the same code path as a database write** and relies on both happening (the dual-write situation), whether or not the word "saga" is used.

Do NOT engage this lens merely because the vocabulary appears. A class named `OrderSaga`, a package called `orchestration`, a `@TransactionalEventListener`, or a message published to a broker is not itself a reason to engage — the trigger is a multi-step unit of work that crosses a consistency boundary and must stay consistent under partial failure, not the words used to label it. **And do NOT engage merely because** a workflow has several steps: a sequence of steps that all commit inside one local transaction against one datastore is not a saga problem at all — it is one transaction, and the right finding is usually to keep it that way.

## Core Principle

A saga is a tool, not an objective. It maintains consistency across a boundary by running a **sequence of local transactions**, each in its own datastore, where a step that must be undone is reversed by an explicit **compensating transaction** — *not* by a distributed ACID transaction and *not* by a database rollback. A saga is justified only when it does concrete work that a single local transaction cannot:

- Keeps a business outcome consistent across aggregates, stores, or services that genuinely **cannot share one transaction**, by sequencing local commits and compensating the ones already done when a later step fails.
- Lets a **long-running** process span waits, callbacks, or human steps without holding a transaction (and its locks) open across them.
- Propagates a business outcome **across contexts** while letting each context own its own data and commit independently.
- Trades the **atomicity and isolation** of a distributed transaction for **availability and looser coupling** — deliberately, knowing what is given up.

That last point is the whole bargain, and it must be made consciously. A saga buys cross-boundary consistency at the price of **eventual** consistency (the system is observably inconsistent between steps), **compensation logic** (every undoable step needs a tested reverse action that may not perfectly restore the prior state), and **lost isolation** (other readers and writers can see and act on the in-between state, producing real anomalies). Distributed ACID via two-phase commit / XA is *possible*, not impossible — a saga is chosen over it on purpose, accepting eventual consistency and writing compensations in exchange for not holding distributed locks and not coupling the participants into one transaction coordinator. None of this is free, and none of it is needed when the work fits in one place.

**If one local ACID transaction — one aggregate, one datastore — suffices, a saga is overengineering.** This is the most common correct finding for this lens. A saga adds eventual consistency, compensation code, idempotency requirements, reliable-messaging plumbing, and a harder testing and observability story; spending all of that on a unit of work a single `@Transactional` method already commits atomically is pure cost. This connects directly to [`./ddd.md`](./ddd.md): **one aggregate per transaction, eventual consistency across aggregates.** A saga is what you reach for *after* DDD has told you the work legitimately spans aggregates and cannot be one transaction — not a substitute for asking whether it needs to span them at all. When the honest answer is "this is one aggregate, one store, one transaction," say so plainly; that is the simpler and safer design. This lens leans hard against dogma: **"no comment" — and "this doesn't need a saga" — are valid, frequent, and correct outcomes.**

## Severity Calibration

Apply these four levels to saga findings (see [`../rules/severity-rubric.md`](../rules/severity-rubric.md) for the shared definitions):

- **MUST** — A consistency or failure-handling decision causes a concrete defect or serious hazard now: a saga step that commits an irreversible side effect (a captured `Payment`, a dispatched `Shipment`) with **no compensation and no retry path**, so a downstream failure leaves the system permanently inconsistent with no way back; a participant that is **not idempotent under at-least-once delivery**, so a redelivered message captures a `Payment` twice or ships an `Order` twice; a **dual write** where the state change and the event are written without a shared transaction, so a crash between them loses the event (downstream never runs) or publishes an event for a change that rolled back (downstream runs for work that never happened); a workflow treated as **one transaction across two services or two stores** where no such transaction exists, so a partial failure silently tears the state; a compensation that assumes it can perfectly restore prior state when it cannot (the value it reverses has already moved), corrupting data. These must be raised and must explain the failure mode.
- **SHOULD** — No defect today, but a specific change would clearly improve correctness-under-failure, recoverability, or comprehension, and you can state the concrete benefit: a choreography whose cross-event ordering is implicit and would be far more traceable as an explicit orchestration before it grows another step; a saga with no timeout or stuck-state handling, so a step that never completes leaves the process hung with nothing watching it; a missing correlation id that will make a failed multi-step flow undiagnosable; an orchestrator beginning to absorb business rules that belong in the participants. Raise it when the benefit is real and nameable.
- **NIT** — Naming of a saga participant, the package an orchestrator lives in, or an optional structural preference with no behavioral consequence (calling a class `OrderSaga` vs `PlaceOrderProcessManager`). Worth a brief note, never a blocker.
- **NO_COMMENT** — The suggestion would be pure taste or speculative architecture: "this should be a saga" / "use orchestration instead of choreography" / "extract a process manager" when the work fits one local transaction, or when both coordination styles are defensible and neither causes a named problem. Stay silent. **"This doesn't need a saga" is the single most common correct outcome of this lens** — and recommending *against* unnecessary saga machinery is itself a finding, not silence.

**Overriding rule:** every finding must name a concrete consequence — what breaks, what gets left inconsistent, what duplicates, what cannot be recovered or diagnosed, or what concretely improves. "This should be a saga" / "use orchestration" / "this isn't a real saga" is **not** a finding. "This captures the `Payment` and then reserves the `Shipment` in a separate service call; if the reservation fails there is no compensation, so the customer is charged for an order that will never ship and nothing reverses it" **is**. If no concrete consequence can be articulated, the finding is NO_COMMENT, no matter how far the design diverges from a textbook saga shape. Style (formatting, naming-case, import order, `this.`, whitespace) belongs to linters, never to review.

## Review Questions

Before raising or accepting a saga (or its absence), the reviewer should be able to answer:

- Does this unit of work genuinely span more than one aggregate / store / service, or does it fit inside one local ACID transaction? If it fits, why is there a saga at all?
- Where is the consistency boundary, and is it acknowledged as **eventual** — is the system's observably-inconsistent in-between state understood and handled, or is the workflow being assumed atomic when it cannot be?
- For each step, what happens on failure? Is the step **retriable** (retried until it succeeds) or **compensatable** (must be undone)? Where is the **pivot** — the point of no return after which the saga must go forward, not back?
- Does every compensatable step before the pivot have a **compensating transaction**, and does that compensation actually undo the *effect* (semantically), accepting it may not restore the exact prior state?
- Is each participant **idempotent**? Under at-least-once delivery a message will sometimes arrive twice — what dedups it so the effect happens once?
- When the workflow both **writes state and publishes an event**, are those in one local transaction (e.g. via an outbox), or are they two separate writes that a crash can split (the dual-write problem)?
- Is the coordination **choreography** (participants react to each other's events, no coordinator) or **orchestration** (a central coordinator drives the steps)? Does the chosen style still let a maintainer trace the end-to-end flow and find where it failed?
- Where does the **saga's own state** live, what are its **deadlines**, and what watches for a **stuck** saga that has neither completed nor compensated?
- Can a failed run be traced across all its steps — is there a **correlation id** threaded through every local transaction and message?
- What **isolation anomaly** does the lack of a distributed transaction expose here (a lost update, a dirty read of in-progress state, a fuzzy read), and is a countermeasure (semantic lock, commutative update, reread, pessimistic ordering) needed or deliberately judged unnecessary?

## Heuristics

### Choreography vs Orchestration

**What to look for:** How the steps of the workflow are coordinated. In **choreography**, each participant reacts to events published by the others and emits its own, with no central coordinator — Ordering publishes `OrderPlaced`, Payment reacts and publishes `PaymentCaptured`, Shipping reacts to that, and so on. In **orchestration**, a central **orchestrator / process manager** explicitly tells each participant what to do, waits for the reply, and decides the next step (or the compensation) — a `PlaceOrderSaga` calls Payment, then Shipping, then Notification, handling each failure. The signal worth acting on is not which style is used but whether the *chosen* style still lets someone trace the flow and recover a failed run.

**Why it matters:** Choreography is decentralized and simple when there are **few steps and few participants** — there is no coordinator to build, and each context only knows its own reactions. But as steps multiply, the end-to-end flow exists nowhere explicitly: it is an emergent property of who-listens-to-what, and tracing "why did this order never ship?" means reconstructing an event chain across services. Orchestration centralizes the flow in one place that is **traceable and where failure handling lives explicitly**, at the cost that the orchestrator can accrete logic and become a god process manager (see Anti-Patterns, and Mediator/Facade in [`./design-patterns.md`](./design-patterns.md)). The concrete consequence of the wrong choice is a workflow nobody can follow (over-grown choreography) or a coordinator that owns rules it shouldn't (over-grown orchestration).

**When NOT to comment:** A two- or three-step choreography among a couple of participants is perfectly fine — demanding an orchestrator for it adds a coordinator and a state store to maintain for a flow that is already traceable. Equally, a small orchestrator that simply sequences a few calls is fine; do not call it a "god process manager" until it actually absorbs business rules. The choice between the two styles is frequently a defensible judgment call with no named consequence either way — that is NO_COMMENT.

**Modern Java/Spring idiom:** Choreography in-process via `ApplicationEventPublisher` and `@TransactionalEventListener`, or across services via a message broker, with each participant owning its reaction. Orchestration as an explicit orchestrator `@Service` that sequences the steps and handles failures, or as an explicit state machine. Spring Statemachine is one way to model the orchestrator's states and transitions explicitly — *verify it is actually a dependency before recommending it; never assume a broker, a state-machine library, or any messaging infrastructure is on the classpath.* Often a plain orchestrator service with an explicit status field is simpler than a state-machine library and needs no new dependency.

**Key review questions:** How many steps and participants does this flow have, and can a maintainer trace the full path today? If it is choreography, where does the end-to-end flow live, and how would you find where a run failed? If it is orchestration, is the coordinator only sequencing, or is it starting to own business rules the participants should own?

**Example review comment:** "SHOULD: the place-order flow is choreographed across five events (`OrderPlaced` → `PaymentCaptured` → `ShipmentReserved` → `InvoiceIssued` → `Notified`), each in a different service reacting to the last. The end-to-end sequence isn't written down anywhere, so diagnosing 'order placed but never shipped' means reconstructing the chain across five logs. Before the sixth step lands, consider an explicit orchestrator that sequences the steps and owns the failure handling — the flow becomes traceable in one place. Weigh it against the coordinator you'd then maintain."

### Compensating Transactions, Retriable vs Compensatable Steps, and the Pivot

**What to look for:** For each step in the saga, whether a failure is handled by **retrying** the step until it succeeds (a **retriable** step) or by **undoing** the steps already done (a **compensatable** step requiring a **compensating transaction**), and where the **pivot transaction** sits — the point of no return after which the saga can only go forward. The strongest signal of a problem is a step that commits a real, externally-visible effect with neither a retry path nor a compensation.

**Why it matters:** A saga has no database rollback across the boundary, so the only way to "undo" a committed local step is a **compensating transaction**: an explicit action that semantically reverses the effect (refund the `Payment`, cancel the `Shipment` reservation, void the `Invoice`). Compensation is **semantic, not transactional** — it undoes the *effect*, and it may not perfectly restore the prior state (a refund is not the same as never charging; a cancelled reservation may have released stock someone else took). Reasoning about failure cleanly means classifying each step: **compensatable** steps (before the pivot) must each have a tested compensation; **retriable** steps (after the pivot) must be safe to retry until they succeed; and the **pivot** is the step after which backward recovery is impossible or unacceptable, so everything past it must be made to complete (by retry), never compensated. The concrete consequence of getting this wrong is a stuck or permanently inconsistent system: a step fails, an earlier committed step has no compensation, and the workflow can neither finish nor cleanly reverse — the customer is charged for an order that will never ship, and nothing fixes it.

**When NOT to comment:** When a step's effect is genuinely reversible and the compensation exists and is tested, there is nothing to flag — do not demand more failure machinery for a flow that already handles its failures. When a step is naturally retriable and idempotent (sending a `Notification`, writing an `AuditEntry`), insisting on a compensation for it is wrong; retrying is the correct handling. Do not invent failure modes the design has already addressed.

**Modern Java/Spring idiom:** Model each step as a local `@Transactional` operation with an explicit, separately-tested compensation operation (in an orchestrator, often a paired `doStep` / `compensateStep`; the step reified as a Command from [`./design-patterns.md`](./design-patterns.md) is a natural fit). Make post-pivot steps retriable with bounded retry and idempotent handlers rather than compensatable. Persist enough saga state to know which steps committed, so compensation runs in reverse order over exactly those.

**Key review questions:** For each step, is failure handled by retry or by compensation, and is that the right choice for that step? Does every compensatable step before the pivot have a compensation, and is it tested? Where is the pivot, and is everything past it made to complete forward rather than relying on an undo that can't happen?

**Example review comment:** "MUST: `capturePayment` commits in the Payment service and then `reserveShipment` is called in the Shipping service; if the reservation fails, nothing refunds the payment. The customer is charged for an order that will never ship and there is no path back to consistency. Either give the payment step a compensating refund that runs when a later step fails, or move payment capture *after* the pivot and make it a retriable step that the saga drives to completion. Note the refund is a semantic undo — it won't be invisible to the customer, which is itself worth designing for."

### Idempotency and Duplicate Handling

**What to look for:** Whether each saga participant produces the **same effect when it processes the same message twice**. The trigger is any state-changing step driven by a message or event under at-least-once delivery: a handler that captures a `Payment`, creates a `Shipment`, issues an `Invoice`, or applies a balance change without first checking whether it already did so for this message or business id.

**Why it matters:** **At-least-once delivery is the norm** for message brokers and retrying callers — a message that was processed but whose acknowledgement was lost will be redelivered, and a retried request will arrive again. If the participant is not idempotent, the duplicate is processed as if new and the effect happens twice: a `Payment` captured twice, an `Order` shipped twice, a balance debited twice. This is one of the most common and damaging distributed-systems defects, and it is silent — there is no error, just a double effect discovered later when the money or the inventory is wrong. Idempotency is therefore not optional for a saga participant; it is a correctness requirement that the at-least-once contract imposes.

**When NOT to comment:** When a handler already dedups by a business or message id (it records the id it processed and ignores repeats), or when the operation is **naturally idempotent** (a state transition to a fixed terminal value, an upsert keyed by business id, a set-to-X rather than add-X), the requirement is met — do not demand a redundant dedup layer. A truly read-only step needs nothing. And exactly-once *delivery* is generally not available from real brokers, so do not propose relying on it; idempotent consumption is how exactly-once *effect* is achieved.

**Modern Java/Spring idiom:** An **idempotent consumer** — record the processed message/business id (a `processedMessageId` row, a unique constraint, or a status check) inside the same local transaction as the effect, and skip if already seen. Carry a stable business key (an `OrderId`, an idempotency key) on the message so duplicates are recognizable. Where the operation can be expressed as an idempotent upsert or a one-way state transition, prefer that to an explicit dedup table. Verify the actual broker and its delivery semantics rather than assuming.

**Key review questions:** Under at-least-once delivery, what happens if this message arrives twice? Is there a business or message id that makes a duplicate recognizable, and is the dedup committed atomically with the effect? Is the operation naturally idempotent, or does it add-on-each-call?

**Example review comment:** "MUST: the `PaymentCaptured` handler debits the customer's balance every time it runs, and the broker delivers at-least-once, so a redelivered message debits twice with no error — the balance silently goes wrong. Make the handler idempotent: record the processed message id (or the `OrderId`) in the same transaction as the debit and skip if it's already been applied. Don't rely on the broker for exactly-once delivery; it won't give it."

### Reliable Messaging and the Transactional Outbox (the Dual-Write Problem)

**What to look for:** A code path that **both writes to the database and publishes a message/event**, relying on both. The signal is a publish call (`ApplicationEventPublisher`, a broker `send`) sitting next to a repository save in the same method, with the two not sharing one atomic write. This is the **dual-write problem**, and it is present whenever a state change is supposed to trigger downstream work via a message.

**Why it matters:** The database and the broker are **two separate systems**; a single local transaction cannot span both. So "save the `Order`, then publish `OrderPlaced`" has two failure windows. If the process crashes after the commit but before the publish, the state change is durable but the **event is lost** — downstream never runs, and the saga stalls with no trigger. If instead the event is published before the transaction commits (or in a transaction that then rolls back), the event is sent for a **change that never happened** — downstream acts on an `Order` that doesn't exist. Either way the state and the messaging diverge, and the divergence is silent. The **transactional outbox** solves it: write the event as a row in an **outbox table in the same local transaction as the state change**, so the event is durable if and only if the state change committed; a separate relay then reads the outbox and publishes to the broker asynchronously, retrying until the broker acknowledges (at-least-once, hence the idempotency requirement above). A common way to run the relay is **change data capture (CDC)** tailing the outbox table or the database log — see the [`cdc.md`](./cdc.md) lens.

**When NOT to comment:** When the side effect is purely in-process and transactional, an outbox is unnecessary machinery — `@TransactionalEventListener(AFTER_COMMIT)` to an in-process handler that itself does only retry-safe work can be enough (note that listener runs *after* commit with no active transaction; see Modernization). When the design already has an outbox or an equivalent atomic publish, do not demand a second mechanism. The outbox earns its keep specifically when a state change must reliably trigger work in another process or service and losing or phantom-publishing the event would break the saga.

**Modern Java/Spring idiom:** An `outbox` table written in the same `@Transactional` method as the aggregate change; a relay (a scheduled poller, or CDC tailing the outbox/DB log) that publishes each row to the broker and marks it sent, retrying on failure. Keep the relay's publish at-least-once and make every consumer idempotent. Verify whether the project already has an outbox, a CDC pipeline, or a broker before proposing one — *never assume a broker or a CDC tool is present.*

**Key review questions:** Does this method write state and publish a message as two separate operations that a crash can split? If the publish is lost after commit, what doesn't run? If the publish happens for a change that rolls back, what acts on data that doesn't exist? Is there an outbox (or equivalent) making the event durable exactly when the state change is?

**Example review comment:** "MUST: `placeOrder` saves the `Order` and then calls the broker to publish `OrderPlaced` in the same method, outside a shared transaction. If the process dies after the save but before the publish, the order exists but Payment is never triggered — the saga stalls with no error. Write the event to an outbox table in the same transaction as the `Order`, and relay it to the broker asynchronously (a poller, or CDC over the outbox — see the forthcoming cdc lens). That makes the event durable exactly when the order is, and consumers stay idempotent for the at-least-once redelivery that follows."

### Lack of Isolation and Countermeasures

**What to look for:** Whether the design accounts for the fact that a saga, unlike a distributed transaction, provides **no isolation** — its intermediate state is visible to, and writable by, other operations while the saga is in flight. The trigger is a saga whose in-progress data can be read or modified by a concurrent actor: an `Order` that is half-processed being read by a report, a `Customer` balance being changed by another flow while a saga is mid-debit, a `Shipment` quantity another saga is also reserving against.

**Why it matters:** Because the local transactions commit one at a time and the saga has no global lock, other transactions can observe and act on the **in-between state**, producing real concurrency anomalies: a **lost update** (two sagas read and write the same field, one overwrites the other), a **dirty read** in the saga sense (a reader sees committed-but-not-yet-finalized state — an `Order` the saga may later compensate away — not an RDBMS uncommitted-data read), and a **fuzzy / non-repeatable read** (a value the saga relied on changes under it before a later step). These are not theoretical — they are the price of trading isolation for availability, and a design that ignores them ships data corruption that surfaces only under concurrency. The standard **countermeasures** are: a **semantic lock** — a status flag marking the record in-progress so other actors know not to treat it as final (and the saga clears or flips the flag on completion or compensation); **commutative updates** — order operations so they produce the same result regardless of interleaving (an additive balance adjustment commutes; an absolute set does not); a **pessimistic view** — sequence the saga's steps so the riskiest update happens in an order that reduces the anomaly window; **reread value / version** — re-read and check a value (or its version) just before acting, an optimistic check that aborts or retries if it changed (a related variant records the operations and reconciles them when they arrive out of order); and **by-value** — choose the concurrency strategy per request based on its risk (a high-value `Payment` gets a stricter strategy than a trivial one).

**When NOT to comment:** When the saga's intermediate state is genuinely not visible or not contended — no concurrent reader or writer touches the records mid-flight — the missing countermeasure costs nothing and demanding a semantic lock is ceremony. When a countermeasure is already present and adequate (a status flag that readers respect, an optimistic version check), do not pile on more. Isolation anomalies are a real but *contended-state* concern; absent contention, NO_COMMENT.

**Modern Java/Spring idiom:** A **semantic lock** as an explicit status on the aggregate (an `Order.status = PROCESSING` that readers and other writers check), cleared on completion or compensation. **Optimistic concurrency** via a JPA `@Version` column for the reread/version-check countermeasure — verify the project uses JPA and that the version column is actually mapped. Prefer **commutative** state changes (relative adjustments) where the domain allows. Avoid holding a pessimistic database lock across saga steps — that reintroduces the long-held-lock problem the saga existed to avoid.

**Key review questions:** While this saga is in flight, who else can read or write the records it touches, and what anomaly does that expose (lost update, dirty read, fuzzy read)? Is there a semantic lock (a status flag) telling other actors the record is in-progress? Are the updates commutative, or version-checked, or is the in-between state simply unprotected?

**Example review comment:** "MUST: while the place-order saga is mid-flight, the `Order` is committed in a `PENDING_PAYMENT` state and the daily revenue `Report` reads all committed orders — so it counts orders the saga may still compensate away (a dirty read), overstating revenue. Add a semantic lock: a status the report query excludes until the saga reaches a terminal state, set on entry and cleared on completion or compensation. If no concurrent reader actually touches in-flight orders, this is moot — but the report does."

### Saga State, Timeouts, and Stuck Sagas

**What to look for:** **Where the saga's own coordination state lives**, whether steps have **deadlines**, and **what detects and resolves a saga that gets stuck** — neither completed nor compensated. The trigger is a multi-step workflow with no durable record of how far it has progressed, no time bound on a step, or no process watching for a run that has stalled.

**Why it matters:** A saga is a state machine that lives across multiple transactions and (often) across process restarts, so its progress must be **durably persisted** — which steps committed, what the current step is, whether it is going forward or compensating. If that state lives only in memory (a thread, an in-flight method), a restart or a crash loses it and the saga is orphaned: earlier steps committed, no record they happened, no way to resume or compensate. Steps that wait on another service or a callback need **deadlines** — a step that never returns will otherwise hang forever, and a saga with no timeout has no way to decide "this isn't coming back, start compensating." A **stuck** saga (a step that failed without triggering compensation, a reply that never arrived, a relay that silently stopped) needs something watching for it — a monitor or a sweep over saga state — or it sits inconsistent indefinitely with nothing surfacing it. The concrete consequence is permanently half-finished business outcomes that no one notices until a customer complains.

**When NOT to comment:** A short in-process saga that completes within one request and is fully covered by retries and an outbox may not need a separate durable saga-state store beyond the aggregate's own status; don't impose a heavyweight state machine on it. Not every step needs an explicit timeout if the underlying call already has one and failure already triggers compensation. Flag the *absence* of state/timeout/monitoring only when a stalled run would actually go undetected and leave inconsistent state — otherwise NO_COMMENT.

**Modern Java/Spring idiom:** Persist saga state explicitly — a saga/process row, or an explicit status on the orchestrating aggregate — so progress survives a restart. Put deadlines on steps that wait (a timeout on the call, a scheduled deadline check). A scheduled sweep (`@Scheduled`) over sagas stuck past a threshold can surface or drive recovery. Spring Statemachine can model the states and persist them — *verify it is a dependency before recommending it; a plain status column plus a scheduled sweep often suffices and adds no dependency.*

**Key review questions:** Where does this saga's progress live, and does it survive a process restart? Do steps that wait on something have a deadline? If a saga stalls — a step fails silently, a reply never comes — what notices, and how is it recovered?

**Example review comment:** "SHOULD: the orchestrator holds the saga's progress in the method's local state and waits synchronously for each service reply. If the process restarts mid-flight, the committed steps are orphaned — there's no durable record to resume or compensate from. Persisting the saga's step state (a status row, or a status on the `Order`) and adding a scheduled sweep for runs stuck past a deadline would make stalls recoverable and visible. Not blocking if these flows are short and a restart can't catch one mid-flight — but worth confirming that's true."

### Observability and Correlation Across Steps

**What to look for:** Whether a single saga run can be **traced end to end** across its local transactions and messages, and whether a failure in the middle is **surfaced** rather than swallowed. The trigger is a multi-step, multi-service flow with no shared identifier threading the steps together, or failure handling that compensates silently with nothing logged or alerted.

**Why it matters:** A saga's work is spread across several services, transactions, and messages, so when it fails "in the middle," the only way to understand what happened is to correlate the steps — and that requires a **correlation id** (the saga id, or a business id like `OrderId`) carried on every local transaction, log line, and message. Without it, a failed run is a scatter of unrelated log entries across services and the failure is effectively undiagnosable; with it, the whole run can be reconstructed and traced. Equally, a saga that **compensates silently** — reverses earlier steps with no log, metric, or alert — hides business-meaningful failures (orders that were placed and then unwound) behind a clean-looking system, so no one learns the failure rate. The concrete consequence of poor observability is incidents that take far longer to diagnose and failure patterns that stay invisible.

**When NOT to comment:** A short, single-service, in-process saga that already runs under one trace context may not need extra correlation plumbing — don't add it for a flow that is already traceable. Don't demand alerting on every compensation if compensations are routine and expected and already counted. Flag observability only when a real failure would actually be hard to trace or a meaningful failure would go unseen — otherwise NO_COMMENT.

**Modern Java/Spring idiom:** Thread a correlation id (saga id / business id) through every step and message, and put it in the logging context (e.g. MDC) so all log lines for a run share it; distributed tracing (Micrometer Tracing / an OpenTelemetry pipeline) propagates a trace across services — *verify the tracing stack is actually configured before relying on it.* Emit a metric or log on compensation so the undo rate is visible. Surface a stuck/failed saga rather than letting it fail quietly.

**Key review questions:** If this saga fails at step three, can someone reconstruct the run across services from the logs — is there a correlation id on every step and message? When the saga compensates, is that surfaced (logged, counted, alerted), or does the business-meaningful failure vanish silently?

**Example review comment:** "SHOULD: the place-order saga spans Ordering, Payment, and Shipping, but no shared id is carried across the steps, so a run that fails at the shipping step shows up as three unrelated log entries in three services with no way to tie them together. Threading the `OrderId` (or a saga id) through every step and message and into the logging context would make a failed run traceable end to end. Emitting a metric when the saga compensates would also make the unwind rate visible. Not blocking, but it'll cut diagnosis time sharply."

## Anti-Patterns

- **A saga where one local transaction would do** — *Diff/design:* a multi-step saga (events, compensations, an orchestrator) built over a unit of work that touches one aggregate in one datastore and could commit in a single `@Transactional` method. *Harm:* the design pays the full saga tax — eventual consistency, compensation code, idempotency requirements, reliable-messaging plumbing, harder testing and observability — for atomicity a single local transaction already provides for free; every reader now traverses coordination machinery to find a plain update, and a window of inconsistency exists where there need be none. *Fix:* use one local ACID transaction; keep the work inside one aggregate per [`./ddd.md`](./ddd.md). Introduce a saga only when the work genuinely cannot be one transaction.

- **A saga step with no compensation for a non-retriable failure** — *Diff/design:* a compensatable step commits a real, externally-visible effect (a captured `Payment`, a reserved `Shipment`) and a later step can fail, but there is no compensating transaction and the failed step isn't retriable to completion either. *Harm:* a downstream failure leaves the system permanently inconsistent with no path back — the customer is charged for an order that never ships, and nothing reverses it. *Fix:* give every compensatable step before the pivot a tested compensation, or move the irreversible effect past the pivot and make it a retriable step the saga drives forward; classify each step explicitly as retriable or compensatable.

- **A non-idempotent saga participant under at-least-once delivery** — *Diff/design:* a state-changing handler (capture `Payment`, ship `Order`, debit balance) driven by a broker message with no dedup, on a broker that delivers at-least-once. *Harm:* a redelivered message processes twice — the `Payment` is captured twice, the `Order` shipped twice — silently, with no error, discovered only when the money or inventory is wrong. *Fix:* make the consumer idempotent — record the processed message/business id in the same transaction as the effect and skip repeats, or express the effect as an idempotent upsert/state-transition. Never rely on the broker for exactly-once delivery.

- **Publishing the event in the same code path as the DB write, with no outbox (dual write)** — *Diff/design:* a method saves state and then publishes a message to a broker (or vice versa) with the two not sharing an atomic write. *Harm:* a crash between the commit and the publish loses the event so downstream never runs and the saga stalls; or the event is published for a change that then rolls back so downstream acts on data that doesn't exist — and both are silent. *Fix:* write the event to an outbox table in the same local transaction as the state change, and relay it to the broker asynchronously (a poller, or CDC over the outbox — see the [`cdc.md`](./cdc.md)); keep consumers idempotent for the at-least-once redelivery.

- **Untraceable choreography** — *Diff/design:* a workflow choreographed across many events where the cross-event ordering and the end-to-end flow are implicit, existing nowhere explicitly, and growing with each new step. *Harm:* the flow is an emergent property of who-listens-to-what; diagnosing "why did this never complete?" means reconstructing an event chain across services, and a missing or misordered listener silently breaks the flow with nothing pinning the sequence. *Fix:* for a flow that has outgrown a handful of steps, make the sequence explicit — an orchestrator that owns the ordering and failure handling — or at minimum document and test the event ordering. This is the Observer / Chain-of-Responsibility visibility hazard from [`./design-patterns.md`](./design-patterns.md) at saga scale.

- **A god orchestrator / process manager** — *Diff/design:* a central orchestrator that, beyond sequencing steps and handling failure, absorbs the participants' business rules — pricing, validation, eligibility — and accretes a method and a dependency per feature. *Harm:* a single high-churn class that knows too much about everyone, hard to test and reason about, a merge-conflict magnet, and a place where the participants' rules hide outside the contexts that own them. *Fix:* keep the orchestrator to coordination — it sequences steps, waits for replies, and drives compensation — and push each business rule back into the participant/aggregate that owns it. This is the Facade-becoming-a-god-service and Mediator-as-god-object anti-pattern from [`./design-patterns.md`](./design-patterns.md).

## Modernization (Java/Spring idioms)

Treat these as the idiomatic modern expressions of saga concepts — but **inspect the target project first and never assume a version, a broker, a state-machine library, or any messaging or CDC infrastructure is present.** Establish the Java language level, the Spring Boot version, the persistence stack, the messaging/streaming technology actually in use (if any), and which libraries are on the classpath before recommending anything. A suggestion the project cannot compile, or that assumes a broker or a tool it does not have, is worse than no suggestion.

Frame every version- or dependency-specific claim as *verify against the actual project; never assume a broker, Statemachine, or library is present.*

- **Choreography via in-process events** — publish with `ApplicationEventPublisher` and react with `@TransactionalEventListener`. Note that **`@TransactionalEventListener(phase = AFTER_COMMIT)` runs after the originating transaction has committed, with no active transaction**, so the listener's side effect is outside that transaction and cannot roll it back; any database write inside it is non-transactional by default and may be silently lost unless the listener opens its own transaction (e.g. `@Transactional(propagation = REQUIRES_NEW)`). Reserve AFTER_COMMIT for retry-safe, external-ish effects — consistent with [`./ddd.md`](./ddd.md)'s domain-events guidance. *Verify the Spring version supports the listener you suggest.*
- **Choreography across services via a broker** — participants react to messages on a message broker. This is where at-least-once delivery, idempotent consumers, and the transactional outbox all become mandatory. *Verify a broker is actually present and confirm its delivery semantics; never assume one.*
- **Orchestration via an explicit orchestrator service** — a `@Service` that sequences the steps, waits for replies, and drives compensation, with the saga's progress persisted (a status row, or an explicit status on the orchestrating aggregate) so it survives restarts. Often the simplest correct form and needs no new dependency.
- **Orchestration via a state machine** — modeling the orchestrator's states and transitions explicitly. **Spring Statemachine** is one option to model and persist saga state — *verify it is actually a dependency before recommending it; never assume it is on the classpath.* A plain status column plus an explicit transition method frequently suffices and adds nothing to the build.
- **The transactional outbox for reliable publishing** — write the event to an outbox table in the **same local transaction** as the state change, then relay it to the broker asynchronously. **CDC** (change data capture tailing the outbox or the DB log) is a common way to run the relay — see the [`cdc.md`](./cdc.md) lens. *Verify whether the project already has an outbox, a broker, and a CDC pipeline before proposing any of them.*
- **Idempotent consumers** — dedup by a message or business id recorded in the same transaction as the effect, or express the effect as an idempotent upsert / one-way state transition, because the broker delivers at-least-once. *Verify the broker's actual delivery guarantee rather than assuming exactly-once.*
- **Correlation ids for tracing** — thread the saga id or a business id through every step and message and into the logging context (MDC); distributed tracing (Micrometer Tracing / OpenTelemetry) can propagate a trace across services. *Verify the tracing stack is configured before relying on it.*

When one local transaction over one aggregate does the job, say so plainly — the saga is not the goal; cross-boundary consistency maintained with the least machinery the problem actually requires is.

## Suggested Comment Style

Keep comments respectful, consequence-first, and severity-honest. Lead with what concretely breaks or is left inconsistent (or what improves), not with a pattern label — name the failure mode so the author can weigh the trade-off rather than feel judged for not following a textbook. State severity honestly; a NIT should read like a NIT, not a blocker. Offer the alternative, and **acknowledge the trade-off the saga makes** — eventual consistency, the cost and imperfection of compensation, the loss of isolation. When one local transaction would do, say so directly and recommend *against* the saga; that is a finding, not silence.

Example openers:

- "What happens to the already-committed `Payment` if this later step fails?" (asks the compensation question)
- "Under at-least-once delivery, if this message arrives twice..." (names the duplicate hazard)
- "This save and this publish aren't in one transaction, so a crash between them..." (names the dual-write failure)
- "This looks like one aggregate, one store — does it need a saga at all?" (recommends restraint)
- "Where does this saga's progress live if the process restarts mid-flight?" (asks the state/recovery question)
- "NIT (not a blocker): ..." (flags taste-level, optional)
- "This flow is short and traceable, and failures already compensate cleanly — no change needed." (explicitly endorses)

Example comments (neutral-domain):

- "MUST: `capturePayment` commits before `reserveShipment`, and a reservation failure has no compensation — the customer is charged for an order that never ships and nothing reverses it. Could we add a compensating refund for the payment step, or move capture past the pivot and make it retriable? The refund is a semantic undo, not invisible to the customer — worth designing for that."
- "MUST: this `PaymentCaptured` handler debits the balance on every delivery, and the broker is at-least-once, so a redelivered message debits twice silently. Recording the processed message id in the same transaction as the debit and skipping repeats would make it idempotent."
- "MUST: `placeOrder` saves the `Order` then publishes `OrderPlaced` outside a shared transaction — a crash in between loses the event and the saga stalls with no error. An outbox row written in the same transaction, relayed asynchronously, makes the event durable exactly when the order is."
- "This is one `Order` aggregate in one store — a single `@Transactional` method commits it atomically. I don't think a saga is buying anything here; it would add eventual consistency and compensation logic for work that's already atomic. I'd keep it as one local transaction unless it's about to span Payment too. (Per the DDD lens: one aggregate, one transaction.)"
- "SHOULD: the flow is choreographed across five events with the ordering implicit — a failed run is hard to trace and a missing listener breaks it silently. Before the next step lands, an explicit orchestrator would make the sequence and the failure handling traceable in one place. Weigh it against the coordinator you'd maintain."

## Integration (architecture-review)

The consuming skill uses this lens as a judgment aid at system/design altitude, not a checklist to enforce:

- **Never comment because a saga is absent.** A design that keeps a multi-step workflow inside one local transaction, or uses choreography rather than an orchestrator, is not a finding by itself. Absence is worth raising only when you can name the concrete consequence (a partial failure that leaves inconsistent state, a dual write that loses an event). And recommending *against* an unnecessary saga — "one local transaction is simpler and safer here" — is a legitimate, frequent finding, not silence.
- **Comment only when there is a real, articulable consequence** — a step that can't be undone, a duplicate effect, a lost or phantom event, an isolation anomaly under concurrency, a stuck run that goes undetected, a failure that can't be traced.
- **Never block on coordination preference.** "This should be a saga" / "use orchestration instead of choreography" / "extract a process manager" without a named consequence is NIT at most, and usually NO_COMMENT.
- **Always tag findings** `MUST` / `SHOULD` / `NIT` per [`../rules/severity-rubric.md`](../rules/severity-rubric.md) so the author can triage by severity. (NO_COMMENT is the silent fourth outcome and, for this lens, the most common one — frequently in the form "this doesn't need a saga.")
- **A few strong findings beat many weak ones.** One well-justified MUST about a missing compensation, a non-idempotent participant, or a dual write is worth more than a list of "consider an orchestrator" observations — drop the weak ones to NO_COMMENT rather than padding the review.
- This lens reasons about cross-boundary consistency, failure and compensation, duplicate handling, reliable messaging, isolation anomalies, saga state, and observability across steps — *system/design altitude*. Aggregate-internal modeling, where the consistency boundary falls, and one-aggregate-per-transaction belong to [`./ddd.md`](./ddd.md); local readability and small refactors belong to [`./clean-code.md`](./clean-code.md); test quality and how a saga's failure paths are tested belong to [`./testing.md`](./testing.md); pattern-level structural calls (Observer for events, Command for reified steps, Mediator/Facade for an orchestrator, Chain of Responsibility) belong to [`./design-patterns.md`](./design-patterns.md). Defer to those rather than re-litigating their concerns here.
